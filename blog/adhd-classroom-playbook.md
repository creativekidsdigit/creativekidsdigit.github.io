## What to Look for in an ADHD Classroom Toolkit

Not all classroom resources are created equal. Some are just pretty templates with no real strategy behind them. Others are so rigid they don't adapt to a child's unique needs.

Here's what **actually works** when supporting a child with ADHD in the classroom:

| **Feature** | **Why It Matters** | **Red Flags to Watch For** |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **Research-Aligned Design** | Tools should follow ADHD best practices (e.g., visual schedules, chunked tasks). | Generic "behavior charts" with no clear structure or evidence-based approach. |
| **Fully Editable & Adaptable** | Every child's needs are different—your toolkit should flex, not force a one-size-fits-all solution. | Static PDFs that can't be personalized; teachers can't tailor them for your child. |
| **Ready-to-Use Resources** | Teachers spend enough time planning—they need **instantly usable** materials. | Vague "printable packs" with no instructions or guidance. |
| **Parent-Teacher Teamwork** | The tools should make it easy for parents and teachers to **communicate and collaborate**. | One-sided tools that leave parents out of the loop. |
| **Executive Function Support** | ADHD impacts planning, focus, and task completion—look for tools that **simplify** these areas. | Overly complex systems that add to the teacher's (and student's) mental load. |
| **Visual & Sensory-Friendly** | Bright colors, clear fonts, and **minimal clutter** help kids with ADHD stay engaged. | Busy, overwhelming designs that distract rather than support. |
| **Classroom-Tested Strategies** | The best tools aren't just pretty—they're **designed by educators** who understand ADHD. | Random Pinterest-style printables without a cohesive system. |
