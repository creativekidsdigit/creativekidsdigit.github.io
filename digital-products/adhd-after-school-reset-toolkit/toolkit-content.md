### PAGE 2 — Why ADHD Kids Fall Apart After School

**Headline:** It's Not Bad Behavior. It's a Drained Battery.

**Opening:**
Your child held it together for 6–7 hours at school. They followed rules, filtered noise, managed social dynamics, suppressed impulses, and maintained focus — all with a brain that makes every one of those tasks 2–3x harder than it is for neurotypical peers.

By 3pm, their self-regulation battery is empty. What happens next isn't defiance. It's collapse.

**Key facts (bullet cards):**
- **After-school restraint collapse** — a recognized phenomenon where children release all the stress they suppressed during the school day
- **Prefrontal cortex fatigue** — the brain's "manager" is exhausted; decision-making, impulse control, and emotional regulation shut down
- **Sensory overload carryover** — fluorescent lights, noise, crowds all day leave the nervous system overwhelmed
- **Transition pain** — switching from "school mode" to "home mode" is a major executive function demand
- **Hunger + dehydration** — many ADHD kids forget to eat/drink enough at school; low blood sugar amplifies dysregulation

**Reframe callout:**
> Your child isn't giving you a hard time. They spent all day giving school a good time — and they have nothing left.

**Checklist ("After-School Struggles — check what you recognize"):**
☐ Explosive meltdowns within 30 minutes of arriving home
☐ Crying over tiny triggers (a sibling's look, a food request)
☐ Refusing to do anything — homework, chores, even fun activities
☐ Screaming, hitting, throwing things
☐ Complete shutdown — going silent, hiding in room
☐ Endless screen-time battles
☐ Inability to transition to homework
☐ Aggressive behavior toward siblings
☐ "I hate school" or "I hate everything" on repeat

### PAGE 3 — The 5 Mistakes Parents Make After School

**Headline:** What Makes It Worse (And What to Do Instead)

**Intro:** These aren't character flaws — they're natural instincts. But for an ADHD child with zero self-regulation left, they backfire every time.

**Five mistake cards (icon + mistake + why it backfires + replacement):**

❌ **Mistake 1: Asking too many questions immediately**
*"How was school? What did you learn? Did you eat your lunch?"*
**Why it backfires:** Questions demand executive function — exactly what's depleted.
**Instead:** Silence. A hug. A snack. Words can wait 30 minutes.

❌ **Mistake 2: Going straight to homework**
*"Let's get homework out of the way first."*
**Why it backfires:** Their brain needs 45–90 minutes to recover before it can focus again.
**Instead:** Decompression first. Homework after the reset window.

❌ **Mistake 3: Offering screens as a "reward" for getting home**
*"Here, watch your iPad while I make dinner."*
**Why it backfires:** Screens provide dopamine but don't regulate. Transition OFF screens triggers a second meltdown.
**Instead:** Low-stimulation decompression first (see Page 6).

❌ **Mistake 4: Lecturing about school behavior at pickup**
*"Your teacher said you didn't finish your work again."*
**Why it backfires:** Shame + fatigue = explosion. They cannot process feedback in this state.
**Instead:** Save school conversations for after dinner, when the brain has recovered.

❌ **Mistake 5: Expecting them to function like a neurotypical child at 4pm**
*"Why can't you just be normal when you get home?"*
**Why it backfires:** They CAN'T. Not won't. Can't. The brain is neurologically depleted.
**Instead:** Lower all expectations for the first 60 minutes. Rebuild from there.

**Closing line (italic, centered):**
*Once you stop fighting the collapse, you can start building around it.*

### PAGE 4 — The 60-Minute After-School Reset System

**Headline:** The Method That Changes Everything

**Intro:** This is the core of the toolkit. A structured 60-minute window between school and the rest of the evening. Same order. Every day. Non-negotiable.

**The 4-Zone Reset (displayed as a horizontal flow with arrows):**

🧘 **Zone 1: DECOMPRESS** *(0–20 minutes)*
Goal: Let the nervous system land.
- No talking, no questions, no demands
- Offer: snack + water + a quiet space
- Options: lie on couch, sit outside, cuddle pet, listen to music
- Parent role: be present but silent. No fixing.

🍎 **Zone 2: FUEL** *(20–35 minutes)*
Goal: Restore blood sugar and hydration.
- Protein + complex carb snack (not sugar crash)
- Water or warm drink
- Eat together if possible — connection without conversation pressure
- Let them eat in silence if they need to

🏃 **Zone 3: MOVE** *(35–50 minutes)*
Goal: Discharge physical tension and reset dopamine.
- Trampoline, bike, running, climbing
- Dance party, obstacle course, ball throwing
- Outdoor time is best (natural light resets circadian rhythm)
- NOT competitive — free play, not structured sport

🤝 **Zone 4: RECONNECT** *(50–60 minutes)*
Goal: Re-establish safety and signal "you're home now."
- 5 minutes of undivided attention
- "Tell me one thing" (not 20 questions)
- Physical affection if they welcome it
- Transition cue: "Okay, we're ready for the next part of the day"

**Callout box:**
> After 60 minutes of this protocol, your child's brain has recovered enough self-regulation capacity to handle homework, chores, or family time. Skip it, and you'll fight the rest of the evening.

**Closing line:**
*This isn't pampering. It's neuroscience. The reset makes everything after it possible.*

### PAGE 7 — How to Prevent Meltdowns Before They Start

**Headline:** Meltdown Prevention Is Easier Than Meltdown Recovery

**Intro:** You can't eliminate every meltdown. But you can reduce them by 70% with consistent environmental design.

**The 6 Prevention Pillars (stacked cards):**

🕐 **1. Predictability**
Same routine. Same order. Same snack spot. Same decompression options. ADHD brains need to know what's coming next — surprise is a threat.

🍌 **2. Blood Sugar Management**
Feed them within 10 minutes of arrival. Protein + fat + complex carb. Low blood sugar = low regulation.

💡 **3. Sensory Reduction**
Dim the lights. Lower the noise. Remove visual clutter from the landing zone. Their nervous system is already overloaded.

📵 **4. Screen Delay**
No screens for the first 45 minutes. Screens provide dopamine WITHOUT regulation — they feel good but don't recharge the brain.

🗣️ **5. Low Verbal Demand**
Fewer words = less processing load. Don't ask. Don't explain. Don't lecture. Just be present.

⏳ **6. Time Buffering**
Build 15 extra minutes into every transition. Rushing an ADHD brain in depletion = guaranteed explosion.

**Callout:**
> You are not walking on eggshells. You are designing an environment that works WITH your child's neurology instead of against it.

### PAGE 8 — Screen Time Boundaries (Without the Battle)

**Headline:** The Screen Problem — And a Plan That Works

**Intro:** Screens aren't evil. But for a depleted ADHD brain, they create a false sense of regulation while actually preventing real recovery. Here's how to set boundaries without nightly wars.

**The Screen Rule Framework:**

**Rule 1: Screens come AFTER the reset, not during it.**
The 60-minute reset happens first. Every day. Non-negotiable. After Zone 4, earned screen time begins.

**Rule 2: Use a visual timer they can see.**
"You have 30 minutes. The timer is on the fridge. When it rings, screens go off." No surprises.

**Rule 3: Give a 5-minute warning before screen-off.**
"5 more minutes, then we'll find a stopping point." This prevents the transition shock.

**Rule 4: Offer a transition bridge.**
After screens off, offer ONE small dopamine hit: a crunchy snack, 2 minutes of a favorite song, a silly joke. Bridge from high-stim to low-stim.

**Rule 5: Keep the boundary even when they rage.**
"I know you're upset. The timer rang. Tomorrow you get screen time again." Hold it. Calmly. Every day.

**What to say when they fight it:**

| They say... | You say... |
|---|---|
| "Just 5 more minutes!" | "The timer decides, not me. Tomorrow you get another turn." |
| "This is SO unfair!" | "I hear you. And the answer is still the same." |
| "I HATE you!" | "You're allowed to be upset. I still love you. Screens are done for today." |
| "Everyone else gets to!" | "In our family, we reset first. That's our rule." |

**Callout:**
> The first 3–5 days will be hard. By day 7, the boundary becomes predictable. By day 14, it becomes routine. Hold the line.

### PAGE 9 — Parent Co-Regulation Guide

**Headline:** How to Be Their Calm When You're Running on Empty

**Intro:** Your child cannot self-regulate at 3:30pm. Their brain literally can't do it alone. They need to borrow YOUR calm nervous system until theirs recovers. That's not indulgence — it's neuroscience.

**The 4-Step Co-Regulation Process:**

1. **Get low** — Physically lower yourself. Crouch, kneel, sit on the floor with them.
2. **Get quiet** — Drop your voice to near-whisper. Fewer words. Slower pace.
3. **Get close** — Offer (don't force) physical contact. Hand on shoulder, sit side by side.
4. **Get patient** — They will not calm in 30 seconds. Give 3–5 minutes of quiet presence.

**What NOT to do during a post-school meltdown:**
- ❌ Ask "what happened at school?"
- ❌ Say "calm down" or "you're fine"
- ❌ Threaten consequences ("If you don't stop, no screen time")
- ❌ Compare to siblings ("Look how calm your sister is")
- ❌ Walk away in frustration (tag-team with a partner if needed)
- ❌ Try to solve the problem immediately

**What to say:**
- "You're home. You're safe."
- "I can see today was really hard."
- "You don't have to talk. I'll just be here."
- "Take all the time you need."
- "We don't have to do anything right now."

**Self-check (callout, for parent):**
> Before you try to regulate your child, check YOUR body:
> - Are your shoulders up by your ears? Drop them.
> - Is your jaw clenched? Unclench.
> - Are you holding your breath? Exhale slowly.
> - Is your voice rising? Lower it by half.
>
> You can't pour from an empty cup — but you can fake calm for 60 seconds. That's often enough.

### PAGE 10 — Sibling Triage Plan

**Headline:** When Two (or More) Kids Need You at Once

**Intro:** The after-school window is hardest when multiple children are dysregulated simultaneously. This page gives you a rapid-response plan for multi-child households.

**The Triage Protocol:**

**Step 1: Separate immediately.**
Physical proximity + dysregulation = escalation. Send each child to a different zone: "You're in the living room. You're in the bedroom. I'll come to each of you."

**Step 2: Safety first, fairness second.**
Who is most escalated? That child gets you first — not because they "win" but because safety comes before justice. The calmer child can wait 3 minutes.

**Step 3: Quick regulation scripts for the waiting child:**
- "I see you. I'll be with you in 3 minutes."
- "You're handling this so well. I'm coming."
- "Here's your snack. I'll check on you in a moment."

**Step 4: Rotate every 2–3 minutes.**
Don't stay with one child for 15 minutes while the other spirals. Short check-ins, back and forth, until both are below boiling.

**Step 5: Reconnect together AFTER both are calm.**
Once everyone is regulated: "That was hard for all of us. We made it through."

**For the non-ADHD sibling (callout):**
> "I know it feels unfair that your brother/sister needs more help right now. That doesn't mean I love you less. It means their brain needs different support. You'll always get your turn with me."

**Practical pre-set:**
- Keep a "sibling survival box" ready: snack, fidget, coloring page, timer
- Pre-assign decompression zones: "After school, [Child A] goes to living room, [Child B] goes to bedroom"
- Reward the waiting sibling: "Thank you for being patient. That earns something special tonight."

### PAGE 13 — Decompression Time Plan (Parent-Facing)

**Headline:** How to Set Up the Decompression Window

**Intro:** The 20-minute decompression window is the single most impactful change you will make. Here's how to protect it.

**Setup checklist:**
☐ Designate ONE decompression spot (couch, beanbag, tent, bedroom corner)
☐ Remove bright lights — dimmer or lamp only
☐ Have snack + water READY before they arrive (no "what do you want?")
☐ Put away homework, schedules, to-do lists — nothing visible that signals "work"
☐ Tell siblings: "This is [Name]'s quiet time. You have yours too."
☐ Set a timer for 20 minutes — when it rings, you gently transition to Zone 2
☐ Turn off your OWN phone if possible — be present without doing

**What decompression is NOT:**
- ❌ Screen time (TV, iPad, phone)
- ❌ Structured play with rules
- ❌ Social interaction they didn't choose
- ❌ A punishment ("Go to your room")
- ❌ Your chance to debrief the school day

**What decompression IS:**
- ✅ Silence or low-volume music
- ✅ Physical comfort (blanket, pet, soft textures)
- ✅ Zero expectations
- ✅ A safe signal: "The hard part of the day is over"
- ✅ Time for the nervous system to shift from sympathetic (fight/flight) to parasympathetic (rest)

**Callout:**
> If your child resists decompression at first: that's normal. They're used to screens as their "off switch." Give the new routine 5–7 days. The nervous system will adapt.

### PAGE 14 — Homework Transition System

**Headline:** How to Move from Reset to Homework (Without a Second Meltdown)

**Intro:** The transition FROM decompression TO homework is the second-highest meltdown trigger of the day. A smooth bridge prevents it.

**The 5-Step Homework Transition:**

⏰ **Step 1: 5-minute warning**
"In 5 minutes, we'll start homework. Finish up what you're doing."

📋 **Step 2: The preview**
"Let's look at what we have tonight." Open the backpack together. List tasks. Let THEM pick which to start with.

🎯 **Step 3: Shrink it**
"We're going to do the FIRST one only. Just that. Then we'll check in." ADHD brains need one task visible, not five.

⏱️ **Step 4: Timer ON**
Set a visual timer for 10–15 minutes. "Work until this rings, then you get a 5-minute break."

🎉 **Step 5: Celebrate the start**
"You sat down. You opened the book. That's the hardest part — and you did it."

**Movement bridge (between reset and homework):**
Before sitting down, do ONE minute of movement: 10 jumping jacks, wall push-ups, a quick dance. This helps the brain shift gears.

**The "I Can't Start" Protocol:**
If they freeze, don't push. Instead:
1. Read the first question aloud for them
2. Write the first word together
3. Say: "Just do 2 minutes. If it's still unbearable, we pause."
4. Shrink → start → expand. Never the reverse.

**Callout:**
> The goal isn't perfect homework. The goal is homework WITHOUT a meltdown. Lower the bar. Raise it later.

### PAGE 15 — After-School Calm-Down Strategies (Kid-Facing)

**Headline:** When Big Feelings Hit After School — Try ONE

**Intro:** After school, your brain is tired. Big feelings will come. That's okay. Pick ONE thing from this list and try it for 60 seconds.

**12 strategy cards (3x4 grid, designed to be cut apart):**

1. 🫁 **Balloon Breath** — Breathe in like you're blowing up a balloon. Hold 3 seconds. Let the air out slowly. Repeat 3x.
2. 🧊 **Cold Reset** — Hold an ice cube or splash cold water on your wrists. Focus on the cold.
3. 🤗 **Butterfly Hug** — Cross arms on chest. Tap one shoulder, then the other. Slow. 10 taps.
4. 🦁 **Lion's Breath** — Big inhale. Open mouth wide, stick tongue out, exhale with a roar.
5. 🧸 **Squeeze It Out** — Grab a pillow or stuffed animal. Squeeze as hard as you can for 10 seconds. Release.
6. 🚶 **Walk It Off** — 3 laps around the house. Or just stand up and stretch tall, then melt down to the floor.
7. 🎵 **Hum Your Song** — Hum your favorite song. Humming calms the inside of your body.
8. 🧱 **Wall Push-Ups** — Push against a wall 10 times, hard. Heavy work tells your brain you're safe.
9. 📝 **Dump It Out** — Write or scribble everything you feel on paper. Nobody has to read it.
10. 🌊 **Wave Breath** — Breathe in for 4. Hold for 2. Breathe out for 6. Imagine ocean waves.
11. 🫧 **Blow Bubbles** — Real or pretend. The slow exhale is the magic part.
12. 🧘 **Pretzel Sit** — Sit cross-legged, wrap arms across chest, squeeze yourself, close eyes. Count to 20.

### PAGE 17 — Meltdown Recovery Steps (Parent-Facing)

**Headline:** When the Meltdown Happens Anyway — Your 5-Step Recovery Plan

**Intro:** Prevention is the goal. But meltdowns will still happen — especially in the first weeks of a new routine. Here's how to move THROUGH it without making it worse.

**The 5 Steps:**

🛑 **Step 1: STOP talking.**
Close your mouth. Seriously. Every word you say during peak dysregulation is gasoline on fire. Silence is intervention.

🧎 **Step 2: Get low and wait.**
Lower your body. Sit on the floor nearby. Signal physical safety without words. Wait 2–5 minutes.

🫁 **Step 3: Breathe audibly.**
Take slow, loud breaths they can hear. In through nose, out through mouth. Their nervous system will start to mirror yours. This is real neuroscience.

💬 **Step 4: First words — validate only.**
When the peak passes (you'll see their body soften): "That was so hard." "Your brain was overwhelmed." "You're safe now."

🤝 **Step 5: Repair and resume.**
After full calm: "What does your body need right now?" Then: gentle return to the routine. No punishment. No lecture. Just resume.

**Timeline callout:**
> Average ADHD meltdown duration: 8–20 minutes.
> Feels like: 3 hours.
> It WILL pass. You do not have to fix it. Just survive it safely.

**What NEVER helps during meltdown:**
- Counting to 3
- Threatening consequences
- Raising your voice to match theirs
- Saying "calm down"
- Asking "why are you acting like this?"
- Physically restraining (unless safety requires it)

### PAGE 20 — ADHD-Friendly Homework Setup

**Headline:** Set the Space. Set Them Up to Succeed.

**Intro:** The right environment can cut homework resistance in half. Set this up ONCE and keep it consistent every day.

**Physical Setup Checklist:**

*The Desk / Workspace:*
☐ Clear surface — ONLY the current task visible
☐ All supplies ready BEFORE sitting down (pencil, eraser, paper)
☐ Water bottle within reach
☐ Visual timer in view (not phone timer — a physical or large-screen one)
☐ Phone/tablet OUT OF THE ROOM (not face-down — gone)
☐ One small fidget available (putty, spinner ring, stress ball)

*The Body:*
☐ Fed and hydrated BEFORE starting (never homework on empty stomach)
☐ Option to stand, kneel, or sit on a wobble cushion
☐ Noise-cancelling headphones OR soft instrumental music
☐ Comfortable temperature (slightly cool is better for focus)

*The Environment:*
☐ Same spot every day (predictability = less resistance)
☐ Warm lighting (lamp, not harsh overhead)
☐ "Do Not Disturb" signal for siblings (sign on door, or visual cue)
☐ Parent nearby but not hovering (body doubling — see below)

**Body Doubling explained (callout):**
> Sit nearby doing YOUR own quiet task — bills, reading, journaling. Don't watch them. Don't correct them. Your calm, focused presence regulates their nervous system and helps them stay on-task. This is research-backed for ADHD.

### PAGE 21 — Focus + Break System (The Sprint Method)

**Headline:** Work in Sprints, Not Marathons

**Intro:** ADHD brains cannot sustain focus for 45 minutes. But they CAN do 10-minute sprints with movement breaks. The key: the break must be EARNED and TIMED.

**The Sprint Method:**

**🟢 SPRINT** (10–15 minutes depending on age)
- Timer visible and counting down
- ONE task only. Cover other assignments.
- No interruptions from parent.
- If they stop, gently redirect: "3 more minutes on the timer."

**🔵 BREAK** (3–5 minutes)
- Physical movement (jumping jacks, dance, walk to kitchen)
- NOT screens (screens make re-engaging harder)
- Set a timer for the break too — it ends when it rings
- Drink water. Use bathroom. Stretch.

**🟢 SPRINT 2** → **🔵 BREAK 2** → **🟢 SPRINT 3** → **DONE**

**Age guide:**

| Age | Sprint length | Break length | Max sprints |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5–6 | 5–8 min | 3 min | 2–3 |
| 7–9 | 10–12 min | 4 min | 3–4 |
| 10–12 | 12–15 min | 5 min | 3–5 |

**If they finish early:** celebrate and STOP. Don't add more work. That teaches them that efficiency is punished.

**If they can't finish:** stop at the timer. Write a note to the teacher. Protect their evening. (See Emergency Plan, Page 26.)

### PAGE 24 — Token System Guide (Parent-Facing)

**Headline:** The Token System — How It Works

**Intro:** Tokens are better than star charts for ADHD kids because they're PHYSICAL. The child can hold progress in their hands. Tangible = real to an ADHD brain.

**Setup:**
1. Choose your tokens: coins, glass beads, Lego pieces, buttons, pom-poms
2. Choose a container: clear jar (so they can SEE progress filling up)
3. Set the earning rules (below)
4. Set the exchange rate (below)
5. Keep it SIMPLE — max 5 earning opportunities per day

**Earning rules (customize to your family):**

| Behavior | Tokens Earned |
|---|---|
| Followed the after-school routine without reminders | 3 |
| Started homework with the timer (no meltdown) | 2 |
| Used a calm-down strategy instead of yelling/hitting | 2 |
| Stayed in decompression zone for full 20 min | 1 |
| Kind to sibling during the after-school window | 1 |
| Screen transition without a fight | 2 |
| Packed backpack for tomorrow | 1 |

**Exchange rate:**

| Tokens | Reward |
|---|---|
| 5 tokens | Choose a snack |
| 10 tokens | 15 extra minutes of screen time |
| 15 tokens | Small toy, book, or craft supply |
| 20 tokens | Special outing (park, ice cream, library) |
| 30 tokens | BIG reward (they choose with parent approval) |

**Rules for success (callout):**
> - Tokens are EARNED, never taken away. Once earned, it's theirs.
> - Give tokens IMMEDIATELY after the behavior (delay kills motivation for ADHD brains).
> - Celebrate the earning: "You just earned 2 tokens! Drop them in your jar!"
> - Don't inflation-adjust mid-week. Keep the rates stable for at least 2 weeks before changing.

### PAGE 25 — Positive Reinforcement Quick Guide

**Headline:** What to Praise, When to Praise, How to Praise

**Intro:** ADHD kids hear more corrections than compliments every single day. Positive reinforcement rebuilds their self-image and makes the system WORK.

**The 3 Rules of Effective Praise:**

1. **Be specific.** Not "good job" — instead: "You sat down and started your homework without me asking. That takes real effort."
2. **Praise the process, not the result.** Not "You got 100%" — instead: "You stuck with it even when it was hard."
3. **Praise immediately.** Don't wait until bedtime. The moment you see the behavior, name it. ADHD brains need instant feedback.

**25 Phrases That Build ADHD Kids Up:**

| For effort | For regulation | For routines |
|---|---|---|
| "You kept going even when it got hard." | "You used your breathing card — that's so smart." | "You followed your routine without reminders!" |
| "That took real brain power." | "I saw you walk away instead of yelling. Wow." | "You're in your decompression zone — great choice." |
| "Look what happened when you didn't give up." | "You told me you were overwhelmed. That's brave." | "Backpack is already packed? You're ahead!" |
| "I noticed how hard you tried." | "Your body wanted to explode but you held it. Amazing." | "Timer went off and you stopped. That's discipline." |
| "You didn't quit. That's your superpower." | "You asked for help. That's strength, not weakness." | "Same routine, every day. You're building a habit!" |

**What to AVOID:**
- ❌ "Why can't you always be like this?" (implies today's baseline is failure)
- ❌ "See? It wasn't that hard." (dismisses their real struggle)
- ❌ "Finally!" (implies they were failing before)
- ❌ "If only you tried this hard every day." (conditional love signal)
- ❌ Comparing to siblings or peers

**Callout:**
> Research shows ADHD kids need a 5:1 ratio of positive-to-corrective feedback to maintain motivation. Five positives for every one correction. Count today.

### PAGE 26 — Emergency After-School Meltdown Plan

**Headline:** When Everything Explodes — Your Emergency Card

**Opening line (gentle):**
Some days, no amount of routine prevents it. The meltdown is here. It's loud. It's physical. It's 3:42pm and you're already exhausted. Here's your emergency card.

**4-Step Emergency Protocol (large, bold, designed as a single printable card):**

🛑 **STEP 1: SAFETY**
☐ Move dangerous objects out of reach
☐ Move siblings to another room
☐ Lower YOUR body (sit or crouch)
☐ Say nothing. Silence is your first tool.

🧘 **STEP 2: WAIT**
☐ Breathe audibly — they'll hear you even if they can't respond
☐ Set a mental timer: most peaks last 5–12 minutes
☐ Do NOT touch them unless they reach for you
☐ If they're destructive: "You can scream. You can't hurt yourself or others."

💬 **STEP 3: RECONNECT (when the peak passes)**
☐ "I'm here. You're safe. That was really hard."
☐ Offer: water, cold washcloth, blanket, stuffed animal
☐ Don't ask "what happened" yet. Just be present.
☐ Physical comfort if they accept it: back rub, hug, hand-hold

🔄 **STEP 4: RESUME (after full calm — 10–20 min later)**
☐ "What does your body need right now?"
☐ Return to the routine at the NEXT zone (don't restart from zero)
☐ No punishment. No lecture. No "we need to talk about what happened."
☐ Tonight's debrief: "What could help NEXT time?" (not "what did you do wrong?")

**Design note:** This should print as a SINGLE, laminate-ready card. Bold. Large font. Numbered clearly. Meant to be grabbed in the moment — like a first-aid card.

### PAGE 27 — Parent Reset Page (Stress Relief for YOU)

**Headline:** You Matter in This Too.

**Opening:**
You cannot regulate your child if you are dysregulated yourself. This page is for YOU — the parent who absorbs the meltdowns, holds the boundaries, and runs on empty by 5pm.

**Your 5-Minute Reset (pick ONE when you're at breaking point):**

🫁 **90-Second Breath Reset**
In for 4. Hold for 4. Out for 8. Repeat 5 times. The longer exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system.

🚪 **The Doorway Break**
Step into another room. Close the door. 90 seconds of silence. You are not abandoning them. You are refilling your capacity.

🧊 **Cold Water Reset**
Run cold water over your hands and wrists for 30 seconds. Splash your face. The vagal response is immediate.

📝 **The Brain Dump**
Grab paper. Write every frustrated thought in your head. Don't filter. Don't censor. 2 minutes. Then rip it up or put it away.

☕ **The Warm Cup**
Make yourself a hot drink. Hold it with both hands. Feel the warmth. Sip slowly. This is your micro-moment of care.

**Affirmations for the depleted parent (6 cards):**
1. "I am doing enough. Even on the hard days."
2. "My child's meltdown is not my failure."
3. "I don't have to be calm every second. I just have to come back."
4. "Asking for help is not weakness. It's wisdom."
5. "They will not remember a perfect parent. They'll remember one who tried."
6. "Tomorrow is a fresh start — for both of us."

**Self-care callout:**
> You are not your child's therapist. You are their parent. If you are burned out, overwhelmed, or at the end of your rope — reach out. A therapist, a support group, a partner, a friend. You cannot do this alone indefinitely, and you should not have to.
