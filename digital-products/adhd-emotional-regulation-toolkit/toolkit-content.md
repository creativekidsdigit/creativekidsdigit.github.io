## PAGE 2 — Why ADHD Kids Feel Everything More Intensely

**Headline:** It's Not Drama. It's Dysregulation.

**Opening:**
ADHD doesn't just affect attention — it affects emotions. ADHD kids feel every emotion faster, stronger, and longer than neurotypical peers. That's not a behavior problem. It's a wiring difference.

**Key facts (bullet cards):**
- **Emotional intensity** — feelings hit harder and faster, like 0 to 100 in seconds
- **Rejection sensitivity** — small corrections feel like personal attacks
- **Slow emotional recovery** — takes 2–3x longer to return to baseline after upset
- **Low frustration tolerance** — minor obstacles trigger big reactions
- **Emotional impulsivity** — acts on feelings before the thinking brain catches up

**Reframe callout:**
> Your child isn't choosing to overreact. Their emotional brake pedal is still developing.

**Checklist ("Emotional Regulation Struggles — check what you recognize"):**
☐ Explosive anger over small triggers
☐ Inconsolable crying that seems disproportionate
☐ "I hate myself" or "nobody likes me" after minor setbacks
☐ Physical aggression when overwhelmed
☐ Shutting down completely (freeze response)
☐ Difficulty naming what they feel
☐ Rapid mood swings throughout the day

**Headline:** Where Do I Feel It In My Body?

**Design:** Simple outline of a child's body (front-facing, gender-neutral) with labeled zones and prompts:

- **Head:** "Fuzzy? Racing thoughts? Headache?"

- **Stomach:** "Sick? Knotted? Empty?"
- **Hands:** "Clenched? Shaky? Hot?"
- **Legs:** "Wobbly? Want to run? Heavy?"

**Instruction:** "Color or circle where you feel the big feeling right now."

**Callout for parents:**
> Teaching body awareness is the #1 predictor of emotional regulation success. If they can locate the feeling, they can learn to manage it.

## PAGE 5 — Calm-Down Strategy Cards (12 Strategies)

**Headline:** My Calm-Down Toolbox

**Intro:** When big feelings hit, pick ONE strategy from your toolbox. Try it for 60 seconds.

**12 strategy cards (3×4 grid):**

1. 🫁 **Box Breathing** — In 4, hold 4, out 4, hold 4. Repeat 3x.
2. 🧊 **Ice Cube Hold** — Hold an ice cube. Focus on the cold until the feeling shrinks.
3. 🤗 **Butterfly Hug** — Cross arms on chest. Tap alternating shoulders slowly.
4. 🦁 **Lion's Breath** — Big inhale through nose. Exhale with tongue out, eyes wide. Roar it out.
5. 🧸 **Squeeze & Release** — Squeeze a pillow/stuffed animal for 10 seconds. Release. Feel the difference.
6. 🎨 **Angry Scribble** — Scribble hard on paper for 30 seconds. Then rip it up.
7. 🚶 **Walk It Off** — 5 laps around the house or 2 minutes outside. Movement resets the brain.
8. 🎵 **Hum Your Song** — Hum your favorite song. Humming activates the vagus nerve (calming system).
9. 🫧 **Blow Bubbles** — Real or imaginary. Slow exhale = nervous system reset.
10. 🧱 **Wall Push-Ups** — Push against the wall 10 times. Heavy work calms the body.
11. 📝 **Write It Out** — "I feel ___ because ___." Put the feeling on paper, out of the body.
12. 🌊 **Wave Counting** — Imagine waves. Count 10 waves rolling in and out. Breathe with them.

**Design note:** Each card should be a rounded rectangle with an emoji/icon, bold title, and 1-line instruction. Designed to be cut apart and kept in a "calm-down kit" bag or box.

## PAGE 6 — Calm-Down Kit Checklist

**Headline:** Build Your Calm-Down Kit

**Intro:** A calm-down kit is a physical box or bag your child can grab when emotions get big. Build it together — let them choose what goes in.

**Checklist:**
☐ Stress ball or putty
☐ Ice pack (for forehead or hands)
☐ Noise-cancelling headphones
☐ Favorite stuffed animal
☐ Journal or scribble paper + crayons
☐ Bubble wand
☐ Weighted lap pad or blanket
☐ Glitter jar (shake and watch settle)
☐ Essential oil roller (lavender or peppermint)
☐ Timer (for timed cool-downs)
☐ "My Calm-Down Cards" (from Page 5)
☐ A photo of someone who loves them

**Where to keep it:**
- Bedroom: under the bed or on a shelf within reach
- Living room: a basket by the couch
- School: a pencil case with small items (putty, breathing card, essential oil)

## PAGE 7 — Coping Skills by Emotion

**Headline:** What to Do When I Feel...

**Four columns, one per emotion:**

**When I'm ANGRY:**
- Squeeze something hard, then release
- Walk away for 2 minutes (not running away — resetting)
- Rip paper or stomp feet (safe physical release)
- Say: "I'm angry and I need space"

**When I'm SAD:**
- Ask for a hug
- Draw how you feel
- Listen to a comfort song
- Say: "I'm sad and that's okay"

**When I'm WORRIED:**
- Name 5 things you can see right now
- Ask: "Is this a NOW problem or a LATER problem?"
- Squeeze your hands and release 3 times
- Say: "I can handle this one step at a time"

**When I'm OVERWHELMED:**
- Close your eyes for 30 seconds
- Remove one thing from your space (less = calmer)
- Ask for help — it's not weakness, it's smart
- Say: "I need a break and I'll come back"

## PAGE 8 — Parent Co-Regulation Guide

**Headline:** How to Be Their Calm (Even When You're Not)

**Intro:** Children under 12 cannot fully self-regulate. They need you to co-regulate first — lend them your calm nervous system until theirs matures.

**The 4-Step Co-Regulation Process:**

1. **Get low** — Physically lower yourself to their eye level. Crouch, kneel, sit.
2. **Get quiet** — Drop your voice. Speak slowly. Fewer words = more safety.
3. **Get close** — Offer (don't force) physical contact. Hand on back, hug, sitting nearby.
4. **Get patient** — They will not calm instantly. Stay present. 2–5 minutes of silence is normal.

**What NOT to do during dysregulation:**
- ❌ Lecture or explain why they're wrong
- ❌ Ask "why are you crying?"
- ❌ Say "calm down" (it does the opposite)
- ❌ Threaten consequences during the storm
- ❌ Walk away in frustration (take turns with a co-parent if needed)

**What to say:**
- "I'm here."
- "You're safe."
- "I can see this is really hard."
- "Take your time. I'm not going anywhere."
- "We'll figure this out after the feeling passes."

## PAGE 9 — Emotional Regulation Scripts for Parents

**Headline:** The Right Words for the Hardest Moments

**Five script boxes:**

💬 **When they're raging:**
- "Your anger makes sense. Let's find a safe way to let it out."
- "I'm going to stay quiet with you until it passes."
- "You can be angry. You can't hurt people or things."

💬 **When they say "I hate myself":**
- "I hear you. That feeling is so heavy."
- "Your brain is being mean to you right now. I don't agree with it."
- "Let me tell you what I see: someone brave who is having a hard day."

💬 **When they're shutting down (freeze):**
- "You don't need to talk. I'll sit here."
- "Blink once if you want me to stay. Blink twice if you need space."
- "Your body is protecting you. That's okay."

💬 **When they recover — the reconnection moment:**
- "Welcome back. I missed you."
- "That was a big feeling. You got through it."
- "What did your body need? Let's remember that for next time."

💬 **When YOU lose your cool (repair script):**
- "I yelled, and I'm sorry. You didn't deserve that."
- "I got overwhelmed too. That's not your fault."
- "Let's start over. I love you."

## PAGE 12 — A Letter to the Parent

**Headline:** Before You Go — A Note for You

**Full-page letter:**

Dear Parent,

If you're reading this, you've already done the hardest thing: you showed up for your child's emotional world instead of running from it.

You didn't get a manual for this. No one taught you how to hold space for a child whose feelings are bigger than their body. No one prepared you for the guilt of losing your temper, the exhaustion of being the calm one, or the heartbreak of hearing "I hate myself" from a seven-year-old.

But here you are. Learning. Trying. Getting back up after the hard days.

That matters. More than you know.

Your child doesn't need you to be perfect. They need you to be present. They need you to come back after rupture. They need you to believe — out loud, over and over — that their feelings are valid and their future is bright.

You're doing that. Right now. With this toolkit in your hands.

One day, they'll regulate on their own. One day, they'll use these tools without being reminded. One day, they'll teach someone else what you taught them.

Until then: grace for them. Grace for you. And tomorrow — a fresh start.

With care,
