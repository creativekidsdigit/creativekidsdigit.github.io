## Page 2 — How to Use This Kit

**Header:** Start Here (You Don't Have to Do It All Today)

**Body:**

This kit is a system, not a checklist. You don't read it cover to cover. You print what you need, when you need it, and ignore the rest until it's time.

Here's the order most families use:

**Tonight (10 minutes):**
- Print pages 3–4. Read the parent guide. That's it.

**This week (30 minutes):**
- Look at the 6-Week Calendar (page 4). Find what week you're in. Print only the pages for that week.

**Two weeks before school:**
- Print the kid-facing pages (12–17) and walk through them together.

**The week school starts:**
- Print the teacher and accommodations pages (18–21). Send what fits your family.

**Once school starts:**
- Use the Weekly Tracker (page 22) every Sunday. Keep it on the fridge.

**If a hard week happens:**
- Pages 16, 17, 23, and 24 are your toolkit. Print one. That's enough.

**A note before you start.** You did not cause your child's struggles, and you cannot solve them in one weekend. This kit will not make ADHD go away. It will give your family a quieter on-ramp into the school year — and a plan for the days when things get loud.

## Page 3 — Why Back-to-School Is Uniquely Hard for ADHD Brains

**Header:** This Isn't Bad Behavior. It's Biology.

**Body:**

Back-to-school season is one of the hardest transitions of the year for an ADHD brain. Six things happen at once.

**1. Sleep debt collides with a new schedule.** ADHD brains already struggle with sleep onset and morning cortisol. A summer of late nights followed by a 6:30am alarm is a chemical shock.

**2. Executive function is being asked to come back online — cold.** ADHD kids spent 8–10 weeks in low-demand mode. Now they're expected to plan, sequence, transition, remember, and inhibit — all day, in a noisy room.

**3. Sensory load triples overnight.** Fluorescent lights, scratchy uniforms, loud cafeterias, hallway crowds, new shoes, new desk. Their nervous system is processing all of it.

**4. Social cognition is on overload.** New teacher. New seating chart. New friend dynamics. New rules. ADHD kids burn massive amounts of working memory just decoding the social environment.

**5. Anxiety often hides as anger.** What looks like a meltdown at 4pm is often unprocessed worry from 9am. ADHD kids frequently lack the language to say "I was scared."

**6. The day ends in restraint collapse.** Holding it together for 7 hours uses every regulation tool they have. Home is the safe place where the dam breaks.

> **The reframe:** Your child is not "being difficult after school." They are doing the hardest cognitive work of their year and bringing the bill home with them.

## Page 5 — The 5 Mistakes Parents Make in August

**Header:** The 5 Mistakes That Make Day One Worse

**Body (rephrased so each is short):**

**1. Going from no schedule to full schedule overnight.** ADHD brains can't pivot that fast. Use the 6-week ramp.

**2. Buying the supplies the school listed instead of the supplies your child can tolerate.** Replace what doesn't work for your kid's sensory system.

**3. Talking about school constantly to "prepare them."** This often raises anxiety. Use the conversation map on page 15 instead.

**4. Skipping the school visit.** Even a parking lot drive-by reduces day-one cortisol.

**5. Treating the after-school meltdown as misbehavior.** It is restraint collapse. Use the recovery plan on page 16.

**Pull-quote:** You can't think your way out of a nervous-system problem. You ramp.

## Page 6 — What ADHD Brains Need in the Final 2 Weeks

**Header:** What Your Child's Brain Actually Needs (Final 2 Weeks Before Day One)

**Body — three columns:**

**MORE OF**
- Predictable sleep onset
- Protein at breakfast
- Outdoor movement
- Visual schedules
- Co-regulation (you stay calm, they borrow it)
- Quiet wind-down hour
- Connection rituals (15 min, screens off)

**LESS OF**
- New activities and outings
- Late nights and big crowds
- Sugar before bed
- Surprise transitions
- Long verbal explanations of "what school will be like"
- Pop-up demands
- Negotiation about non-negotiables

**KEEP THE SAME**
- Their favorite calming tools (fidgets, blanket, music, pet)
- Their favorite foods
- Their bedtime story or audiobook
- The adult who already co-regulates them best

## Page 7 — Parent Mindset Reset

**Header:** A 60-Second Script to Read Before Day One

**Body:**

Read this on the drive to school, or in the bathroom before the bus comes.

> My child's brain is doing the hardest cognitive work of the year today.
>
> What I will see at 3pm is not who they are.
> It is the bill for what they held together all day.
>
> My job today is not to fix anything.
> My job is to be the calm place they crash into.
>
> I will lower my voice before I raise my standards.
> I will lower my pace before I lower my expectations.
> I will offer a snack before I offer a question.
> I will offer a hug before I offer a correction.
>
> I am not failing.
> They are not broken.
> We are doing something hard, together.

**Footer line:** Save this on your phone. Re-read every September Monday.

## Page 8 — Week 6: Sleep Reset Protocol

**Header:** Week 6 — Sleep Reset Protocol

**Subhead:** Two weeks earlier than you think. ADHD brains take longer to drop in.

**Body — the protocol:**

**Step 1 — Find the gap.** What time does the bus come? Subtract 11 hours (kids 6–10) or 10 hours (kids 11–16). That's the bedtime target.

**Step 2 — Move in 10-minute increments.** Every 2 to 3 nights, shift bedtime 10 minutes earlier. Move wake-up at the same pace.

**Step 3 — Add 60 minutes of low input.** Lights down. No screens. Audiobook, drawing, lego, bath, story.

**Step 4 — Anchor a 3-step wind-down.** Same 3 actions, same order, every night. Brush. Book. Lights low. (Or: shower, music, hug.)

**Step 5 — Track for 7 days, then adjust.** Use the tracker box below.

**Tracker grid (printable):**

**Footer note:** If your child has been on a stimulant medication and the prescriber has advised a "med holiday" or schedule change for the school year, plan that conversation now. Do not change medication on the first week of school.

## Page 10 — Week 4: ADHD-Friendly Backpack & Locker Setup

**Header:** Week 4 — Backpack & Locker Setup

**Subhead:** Visual systems beat memory every time.

**Body:**

**Backpack rules (build it together):**

1. **One zone per class.** Color-code folders to subjects. Same color stays together.
2. **One pocket only for "must come home."** Lunchbox, planner, and signed papers. Same pocket every day.
3. **One pocket only for "stays at school."** Pencil case, supplies. Doesn't move.
4. **A laminated mini-schedule** clipped to the inside flap.
5. **One regulation tool** in an outside pocket — fidget, gum (if allowed), photo card.

**Locker / cubby setup:**

- Magnetic mini-whiteboard with the day's reminders.
- Photo of the family on the inside door.
- A small basket for "papers to bring home" — empties into the backpack at the last bell.
- A spare snack and a backup pencil (low-cost, high-rescue).

**Visual checklist printable for the inside flap:**

> **My Backpack — Going Home**
> ☐ Lunchbox in the front
> ☐ Papers in the front
> ☐ Pencil case in the back
> ☐ Water bottle clipped on
> ☐ Library book if it's Tuesday

## Page 11 — Week 3: Anchor Routine Builder

**Header:** Week 3 — Anchor Routine Builder

**Subhead:** Five steps. Same order. Every day. That's the whole rule.

**Body:**

**The morning anchor (5 steps total — pick the ones that fit your family):**

1. **Wake + light.** Open blinds, lamp on, no phone yet.
2. **Body first.** Bathroom, water, 5 jumping jacks or stretches.
3. **Fuel.** Protein-forward breakfast. Same 2–3 options on rotation.
4. **Dress.** Clothes set out the night before.
5. **Launch.** Backpack, shoes, hug, out the door.

**The after-school anchor (5 steps):**

1. **Decompress.** No questions for 20 minutes. Snack + screen or quiet activity.
2. **Movement.** 15–20 minutes of any body-based reset.
3. **Connection.** 10 minutes 1:1 with you, screens off.
4. **Homework window.** Short, timed, with breaks. (Use the Homework Survival Toolkit if you have it.)
5. **Wind-down.** Bath / book / music. No new demands.

## Page 15 — The "I'm Worried About School" Conversation Map

**Header:** The "I'm Worried About School" Conversation Map

**Subhead:** A 5-step script for the night-before-school anxiety spiral.

**Body:**

**Step 1 — Listen without fixing (2 minutes).**
> "Tell me what your brain is saying about school."

**Step 2 — Name the feeling.**
> "It sounds like part of you feels [worried / scared / nervous / mad]. That makes sense."

**Step 3 — Find the specific worry.**
> "What is the worst thing your brain thinks could happen tomorrow?"

**Step 4 — Build a tiny plan for that specific thing.**
> "If that happened, here's who you'd find. Here's what you'd say. Let's practice once."

**Step 5 — Anchor the body.**
> "Three slow breaths. Hand on your chest. I've got you. We don't solve more tonight."

**Don't say (these usually backfire):**
- "You'll be fine."
- "Don't worry about it."
- "Other kids do it every day."
- "When I was your age..."

**Do say:**
- "That makes sense."
- "Tell me more."
- "What would help right now?"
- "I'm here. I'm not going anywhere."

## Page 16 — First-Week Recovery Plan (Decompression Dial)

**Header:** First-Week Recovery — The Decompression Dial

**Subhead:** Pick a level for the afternoon based on what your child can handle.

**Body — three levels:**

**LEVEL 1 — GREEN (regulated, social, hungry but calm)**
- Snack + 20 min decompression
- 30 min activity of their choice
- Light homework (if any)
- Normal evening rhythm

**LEVEL 2 — YELLOW (snappy, withdrawn, hyper-bouncy, weepy)**
- Snack first, no questions
- 45 min total decompression
- Movement before talking
- Homework deferred or shortened
- Earlier bedtime by 30 min

**LEVEL 3 — RED (restraint collapse: meltdown, shutdown, refusal, rage)**
- Snack + drink, dim lights, low voice
- No demands for 60–90 min
- Co-regulate; don't teach
- Skip homework tonight
- Skip evening commitments
- Notify the teacher tomorrow that today was rough

**Parent rule for the first week:** Default to one level higher than you think. The school day is harder than it looks.

## Page 19 — ADHD Accommodations Menu

**Header:** ADHD Accommodations Menu

**Subhead:** A menu of what often helps ADHD students. Share with your teacher or 504/IEP team. Highlight the ones you'd like to try.

**Body — 5 categories:**

**ENVIRONMENT**
- Preferential seating (away from doors, windows, high-traffic)
- Reduced visual clutter on desk
- Noise-reducing headphones during independent work
- Access to a quiet "reset" spot

**INSTRUCTION**
- Instructions given written + verbally
- Multi-step directions broken into one step at a time
- Check-in for understanding before independent work begins
- Visual schedule of the day posted

**ASSIGNMENTS**
- Extended time on tests and longer assignments
- Shortened written work where comprehension can be assessed differently
- Permission to type instead of handwrite
- Use of a graphic organizer or template

**REGULATION**
- Movement break every 20–30 minutes
- Approved fidget tools
- Permission to stand or use a wobble cushion
- A non-verbal "I need a break" cue with the teacher

**EXECUTIVE FUNCTION**
- Daily homework written down by teacher or with peer
- Visual checklist for end-of-day backpack pack-up
- Weekly check-in on missing work without public callout
- Two reminders before transitions

**Footer line:** None of these are "extras." They are scaffolds that allow your child's intelligence to show up on the page.

## Page 20 — IEP / 504 Quick-Reference Card for Parents

**Header:** IEP vs. 504 — A Parent's Quick Reference

**Body — two-column comparison:**

**504 PLAN (Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act)**
- Civil rights law. Provides accommodations.
- Eligibility: a disability that "substantially limits" a major life activity (including learning, focus, behavior).
- No specialized instruction; only access supports.
- Lighter paperwork. Often quicker to put in place.
- Reviewed annually (typically).
- Best if: your child needs accommodations but is making academic progress.

**IEP (Individualized Education Program — IDEA)**
- Special education law. Provides accommodations + specialized instruction + measurable goals.
- Eligibility: meets criteria under one of 13 IDEA categories (e.g., Other Health Impairment for ADHD) AND requires specialized instruction.
- Includes services like speech, OT, resource room, etc.
- More paperwork; legally enforceable goals.
- Reviewed annually; reevaluation every 3 years (typically).
- Best if: your child needs more than accommodations to access learning.

**Your rights as a parent (general):**
- You can request an evaluation in writing at any time.
- The school must respond in writing within a defined timeline.
- You are an equal member of the IEP/504 team.
- You can bring a support person to any meeting.
- You can disagree, in writing, with parts of the plan.

**Three things to do this year:**
1. Keep a folder (paper or digital) of every email, plan, report card, and meeting note.
2. Send all important requests in writing — even if you also said it verbally.
3. Re-read the plan in October, January, and April. Ask: "Is this still working?"

**Footer disclaimer:** General information; not legal advice. Laws and processes vary by country, state, and district. For binding guidance, consult an education attorney or advocate.

## Page 24 — Emergency School Refusal Plan

**Header:** When My Child Won't Go to School

**Subhead:** A calm protocol for the morning the wheels come off.

**Body:**

**First — what this is.** School refusal is a nervous-system signal, not a behavior choice. The goal of this protocol is not to "win" the morning. It is to keep the relationship intact and gather information.

**Step 1 — Lower the stakes for THIS morning.**
- Drop the volume of your voice.
- Sit down at the same level as your child.
- Soften your face.
- Stop trying to leave the house for 5 minutes.

**Step 2 — Find the "what" before the "why."**
- "Your body is saying no. Tell me what your body is doing."
- Don't ask "why don't you want to go?" yet — they often don't know.

**Step 3 — Offer a smaller version of the day.**
- "Can we go for the first hour and reassess?"
- "Can we go in late after we eat together?"
- "Can I drive you instead of the bus today?"

**Step 4 — Decide your floor.**
- If your floor is "they go today," stay calm but firm.
- If your floor is "we keep the relationship and sort this out," stay home and use the day to investigate.
- Either is a valid parenting choice; pick before you escalate.

**Step 5 — Investigate that day.**
- Email the teacher and ask if anything happened in the last 3 school days.
- Check sleep, food, social, sensory, and academic factors from the past 72 hours.
- If refusal happens 3+ days in a row, contact the school counselor and your child's pediatrician or therapist.

**What NOT to do:**
- Bribe big ("a new toy if you go").
- Threaten ("you'll lose ___ if you don't").
- Lecture during the refusal.
- Force physically except in cases of safety.

**Pull-quote:** A child refusing school is not a child being difficult. They are a child whose nervous system has run out of tools.

**LAYOUT:** Five-step protocol. "What NOT to do" as a small terracotta-tinted box. Pull-quote in large italic across the bottom.
