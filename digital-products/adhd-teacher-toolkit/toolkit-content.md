# SECTION 1 — UNDERSTANDING ADHD IN THE CLASSROOM

---

## PAGE 6 — WHAT ADHD ACTUALLY IS (NEUROLOGICALLY) 🧭

**Forget the "kids who won't sit still" definition. It is wrong.**

ADHD is a difference in three brain systems:

**1. The dopamine system (reward + motivation).**
ADHD brains under-produce or under-use dopamine. Dopamine is what makes "boring but important" tasks feel doable. Without enough of it, a student is not lazy — their brain is *literally not generating the chemical signal* that says "this is worth doing."

**2. The prefrontal cortex (executive function).**
The PFC manages planning, working memory, impulse control, time perception, and emotional regulation. In ADHD students, the PFC develops 2–3 years behind peers. A 9-year-old with ADHD has the executive function of a 6- or 7-year-old. Their math may be at grade level. Their *ability to manage themselves* is not.

**3. The default mode network (mind-wandering).**
In neurotypical brains, the DMN turns off when you focus. In ADHD brains, it stays partly on — which is why your student is "with you" and "gone" at the same time.

**The classroom translation:**

- "Lazy" usually means *cannot generate dopamine for this task right now.*
- "Forgetful" usually means *working memory loaded past capacity.*
- "Defiant" usually means *emotional flood with no impulse brake.*
- "Disorganized" usually means *time and space perception is impaired.*
- "Daydreamer" usually means *DMN didn't fully switch off.*

None of this is a character flaw. All of it is treatable, accommodatable, and predictable once you see the pattern.

📚 *For deeper reading: Russell Barkley's work on executive function; Thomas E. Brown's six-cluster model; CDC ADHD basics.*

---

## PAGE 7 — THE THREE PRESENTATIONS 🧭

ADHD is not one thing. The DSM-5 recognizes three presentations:

| Presentation | What it looks like in class | Most often missed in |
|---|---|---|
| **Predominantly Inattentive** | Quiet. Stares out the window. Loses things. Misses directions. "Spacey." Often a strong reader who can't finish a worksheet. | Girls and quiet boys — often goes undiagnosed until middle school or later. |
| **Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive** | Blurts. Moves constantly. Out of seat. Touches everything. Talks over peers. Often labeled "behavior problem" by 1st grade. | Young boys — over-referred for discipline, under-referred for evaluation. |
| **Combined Type** | Both clusters. The most common presentation overall. | Anyone — but symptoms vary widely day to day. |

**Two things teachers consistently miss:**

1. **Inattentive ADHD looks like "she just needs to try harder."** It is not. She is trying *much* harder than her peers to produce the same output. The exhaustion is real.
2. **Hyperactive symptoms fade with age; inattentive symptoms do not.** A bouncy 2nd grader often becomes a daydreaming, "underachieving" 7th grader. Same ADHD, different cover.

**Movement does not equal ADHD.** A child who moves a lot and *learns well* is just a kinesthetic kid. A child who moves a lot and *cannot complete grade-level work that they understand* is showing ADHD impairment. The diagnostic line is functional impact, not activity level.

## PAGE 8 — 12 BEHAVIORS THAT LOOK LIKE DEFIANCE BUT ARE ADHD 📎

🖨 *Print this and tape it inside your teacher binder. Read it before any discipline conversation.*

| The behavior you see | What it usually is | What to try first |
|---|---|---|
| Ignores a direct instruction | Did not hear it — DMN was on. Or heard it, lost it from working memory in 4 seconds. | Get attention first, name the student, give *one* step, ask them to repeat it back. |
| "Talks back" or argues | Emotional flood + no impulse brake; sometimes Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria. | Disengage. Defer the conversation 10 minutes. Return privately. |
| Refuses to start work | Task initiation deficit; the gap from "I should start" to "I am starting" is broken. | Start the first line *with* them. Sit beside, do the first problem together. |
| Loses materials repeatedly | Working memory + time perception. | One folder, one place, one routine. Replace, don't punish. |
| Submits incomplete work | Lost track of time. May not know how much time has passed. | Visible timer + a "stop and submit at the buzzer" cue. |
| Calls out without raising hand | Impulse — the thought left the mouth before the brake engaged. | Hand signal system. Reward the *attempt* to inhibit. |
| Walks around during lesson | Sensory + dopamine seeking. | Movement breaks built into the lesson. Standing desk option. |
| Cries or melts down at small frustrations | Emotional dysregulation — Brown's 4th cluster. | Pre-teach a "pause card." Lower demand, do not raise it. |
| Doesn't bring homework back | Forgetting + organization, not avoidance. | Email parents that day's HW; use a single homework folder. |
| Stares into space | DMN didn't switch off. Not daydreaming "by choice." | Reset attention — touch the desk, say their name softly, point. |
| Is rude after a redirection | Shame spike → RSD reaction. | Praise the *behavior* you want next, not the one you don't. Move on. |
| Sleeps in class | Often ADHD-related sleep disorder; sometimes medication wearing off. | Note time of day. Loop in parents. Do not punish. |

**The reframe rule:** *Before issuing a consequence, ask: "Is this a won't or a can't?"* If you cannot say with certainty that it's a "won't," treat it as a "can't" and accommodate first.

## PAGE 9 — WHY ADHD STUDENTS FAIL IN CLASSROOMS DESIGNED FOR NEUROTYPICAL BRAINS 🧭

The traditional classroom asks for six things at once:

1. Sit still.
2. Hold the instruction in your head while you start it.
3. Track time without a clock.
4. Manage your own materials.
5. Inhibit impulses (don't blurt, don't move).
6. Regulate emotion when you're frustrated.

A neurotypical 8-year-old can do five out of six on a good day. An ADHD 8-year-old can do one or two — and not always the same one or two.

**The hidden tax.**

By the time an ADHD student reaches the *content* of your lesson, they have already spent more cognitive fuel on self-regulation than their peers will spend all day. By 11 a.m., the tank is dry. The afternoon meltdown is not new behavior; it is *fuel exhaustion*.

**The fix is not "more discipline."**

It is *reducing the regulation load* so that the student has enough fuel left to learn. This is not lowering the bar. It is changing the path to the bar. The accommodations in Section 2 are your tools for this.

> "Kids do well if they can."
> — Ross Greene, *The Explosive Child*

If a student is not doing well, something is in the way. Your job is to find it. This toolkit is the search kit.

## PAGE 10 — THE 7 EXECUTIVE FUNCTION DOMAINS EXPLAINED 🧭

Most teachers learn about "executive function" as a vague blob. It is not. There are seven distinct domains, and ADHD impairs each one differently.

| # | Domain | Plain-English definition | Classroom impact when impaired |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Response inhibition** | Pausing before reacting. | Blurting, interrupting, physical impulsivity. |
| 2 | **Working memory** | Holding info in mind while doing something. | Loses multi-step directions, forgets what they were doing mid-task. |
| 3 | **Emotional control** | Regulating feelings to keep going. | Meltdowns, shutdowns, rage at small frustrations. |
| 4 | **Sustained attention** | Maintaining focus over time. | Drifts after 5–10 minutes; appears to "tune out." |
| 5 | **Task initiation** | Starting something even if you don't feel like it. | Stares at blank page; needs an adult to "start" with them. |
| 6 | **Planning/prioritization** | Sequencing steps; knowing what matters. | Long-term projects unravel; "doesn't know where to start." |
| 7 | **Organization** | Managing materials, space, info. | Backpack is a black hole; loses pencils daily. |

A subset of writers add: **Time management, Flexibility (set-shifting), Metacognition, and Goal-directed persistence.** All of these are also impaired in ADHD, often more than the "core" seven.

🧠 *Why it matters for teaching:* an accommodation that targets working memory (e.g., visual checklist) will not help a student whose primary deficit is task initiation. Match the support to the deficit. The Accommodation Selection Worksheet (page 21) walks you through this.

## PAGE 11 — COMMON COMORBIDITIES 🧭

About 60–80% of students with ADHD have at least one additional diagnosis. You will rarely see "clean" ADHD in your room.

| Comorbidity | How often it co-occurs with ADHD | What it adds in the classroom |
|---|---|---|
| **Anxiety** | ~30–40% | Perfectionism, freezing, school refusal, somatic complaints (stomachaches). |
| **Learning Disability** (esp. dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia) | ~25–40% | A real academic gap — not "won't try," can't currently access. |
| **Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)** | ~40% of boys with ADHD by adolescence | Open defiance, argumentativeness. Often a *symptom* of unmet ADHD needs, not a separate problem. |
| **Autism Spectrum Disorder** | ~30–50% overlap | Rigidity, sensory dysregulation, social misreads alongside ADHD symptoms. |
| **Depression** | Rises with age — common in middle school | Withdrawal, flat affect, drop in already inconsistent work. |
| **Sleep disorders** | ~50% | Morning shutdown, afternoon crash, irritability. |
| **Tic disorders / Tourette's** | ~10–20% | Vocal or motor tics that increase under stress. |

**Two things to remember:**

1. **You are not diagnosing.** You are noticing. Your notes feed a clinician's evaluation.
2. **Treating only ADHD when anxiety is the real driver of the day's behavior will fail.** Loop in the counselor early.

## PAGE 12 — ADHD BY AGE AND GRADE LEVEL 🧭

| Stage | What's typical | What teachers most often see |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-K / K** | Hyperactivity is normal; ADHD is hard to distinguish from age. Look for *severity* and *impairment*. | Constant movement, aggression in transitions, inability to do circle time. |
| **Grades 1–2** | EF lag becomes visible. Reading + writing demand increases. | Refuses to write, melts down at desk work, "behavior" reports begin. |
| **Grades 3–5** | The "4th-grade slump." Demands shift from learning to *organizing* learning. Inattentive girls finally surface. | Missing homework, lost folders, drop in test scores despite known content. |
| **Middle school (6–8)** | Multiple teachers + lockers + agendas = executive function nightmare. Self-esteem dips hard. | Failing grades despite passing tests; "doesn't care" presentation; social withdrawal. |
| **High school (9–12)** | Independence demand exceeds EF capacity. Co-occurring anxiety/depression rises. | Procrastination, late work, missed deadlines, possible substance experimentation. |

**Grade-level note:** the *moment* of greatest visible impairment is usually the year the organizational demand jumps. For most students, that is 3rd–4th grade and again at the 6th-grade middle school transition. Plan extra scaffolding at those years.

## PAGE 14 — THE ACCOMMODATION HIERARCHY 🧭

Accommodations are *not* favors. They are environmental and instructional adjustments that change the path to grade-level work without changing the destination.

Work them in three tiers — universal first, individualized only when needed.

### Tier 1 — Universal (whole class)

Things every student benefits from. ADHD students need them; neurotypical students get more out of them.

- Visual daily schedule on the board.
- "First / then" cards visible during transitions.
- A movement break every 25–30 minutes (elementary) or every 45 minutes (middle).
- Directions given out loud *and* written.
- Visible classroom timer for any task longer than 5 minutes.
- A "calm corner" available to anyone, no permission required.

### Tier 2 — Targeted (small group / at-risk students)

For students showing ADHD-like patterns but without a formal plan yet.

- Preferential seating (front, side, low-distraction).
- Modified materials (fewer items per page).
- Check-in / check-out with one trusted adult.
- Daily home-school sheet.
- Movement role (paper passer, message runner).

### Tier 3 — Individualized (504 / IEP)

Documented accommodations tied to a formal plan.

- Extended time (typically 1.5x).
- Tests in a separate setting.
- Use of fidget tools.
- Scribe / speech-to-text.
- Reduced number of problems with proof of mastery.
- Frequent check-ins from a designated staff member.

**Rule:** never use a Tier 3 accommodation when a Tier 1 or 2 change would solve the problem. It is more sustainable and more equitable.

## PAGE 15 — ENVIRONMENTAL ACCOMMODATIONS 📋

These adjust the *physical space* of the classroom.

| Accommodation | What it solves | How to set it up |
|---|---|---|
| Preferential seating (front, near teacher, away from window/door) | Reduces visual distraction; increases proximity prompts. | Map your room. Place the highest-need student where *you* stand most often. |
| Low-distraction seat option (carrel, corner desk) | Helps when student is overstimulated. | Designate one. Frame as "focus office" — available to everyone. |
| Standing desk or wobble cushion | Channels need for movement. | One standing-height table or two wobble cushions. Available without permission. |
| Tennis balls on chair legs | Reduces auditory distraction from chair-scraping. | Free; ask the office. |
| Visual schedule posted at student eye level | Reduces "what's next?" anxiety. | Velcro icons or magnetic strip. Update each morning. |
| Materials station near student desk | Reduces transitions for forgotten items. | A small caddy: spare pencil, extra paper, fidget, water. |
| Quiet zone / privacy folder during work | Reduces visual + auditory load during independent work. | Tri-fold privacy boards; cardboard from any office store. |
| Headphones (noise-reducing) | Reduces auditory overwhelm. | Provide a class set; no stigma. |
| Bin under desk for backpack | Reduces clutter, lost-item triggers. | One milk crate per desk. |
| Visible analog clock + digital timer | Builds time perception. | Both within the student's line of sight from their seat. |

📌 *Implementation tip:* roll these out for the whole class. ADHD students get the help they need without the spotlight. Equity-friendly. Stigma-free.

## PAGE 16 — INSTRUCTIONAL ACCOMMODATIONS 📋

These adjust *how* you deliver lessons.

| Accommodation | What it solves | How to do it |
|---|---|---|
| Get attention before instruction | Working memory only encodes what was attended to. | Name first. Wait for eye contact (or a hand on the desk for kids who avoid eye contact). Then speak. |
| One direction at a time | Overload. | Break multi-step directions into single beats. Repeat back. |
| Written + verbal directions | Reinforces working memory. | Put the multi-step on the board. Refer back to it visually. |
| "I do / we do / you do" model | Bridges task initiation gap. | Always model first. Always do the first problem together. |
| Frequent comprehension checks | ADHD students may miss the *beginning* of instruction. | Every 5 min: "Tell me the next step you're doing." |
| Chunk lessons into 10-minute segments | Sustained attention is limited. | Lesson → activity → lesson → activity. Avoid lecture > 12 min. |
| Cue cards for transitions | Transitions are the highest-risk time. | Visual countdown (5–4–3–2–1) for ending an activity. |
| Pre-teach vocabulary | Reduces in-the-moment working-memory load. | Frontload 5 key words the day before. |
| Provide notes or guided notes | Reduces dual-task burden (listen + write). | Cloze notes with key words missing. |
| Allow non-verbal participation | Verbal demand can be overwhelming. | Thumb-up/thumb-down responses. Hand signals. Whiteboards. |

🧠 *The 30-second rule:* if a multi-step direction can't be repeated back by the student in their own words within 30 seconds, it was too many steps.

## PAGE 17 — ASSIGNMENT & WORKLOAD ACCOMMODATIONS 📋

These adjust the *quantity and shape* of the work.

| Accommodation | What it solves | How to do it |
|---|---|---|
| Reduce number of problems | Mass demand triggers shutdown. | Cross out alternating problems. Mastery shown with fewer items. |
| Chunk worksheets into halves | Visual overload of a full page. | Fold the paper. Reveal one section at a time. |
| Shorter response options | Writing demand exceeds output capacity. | Multiple choice or fill-in instead of long-form. |
| Allow typing instead of writing | Bypasses dysgraphia + slow handwriting. | Chromebook, iPad, or scribe. |
| Break long-term project into checkpoints | Planning + prioritization is impaired. | Calendar with weekly milestones. Adult check on each. |
| Provide a graphic organizer | Sequencing demand. | Use story maps, T-charts, flowcharts. |
| Allow oral responses for content | Writing speed ≠ knowledge. | Record on Flip / Seesaw / a phone. |
| Reduce copying from board | Working memory load + visual tracking. | Provide a printed copy. |
| Accept work in alternative formats | Output flexibility. | Voice notes, drawn responses, video. |
| Frequent submission, not weekly | Procrastination + time blindness. | Submit each section as completed. |

**The 50% rule.** If a student is on grade level cognitively but cannot complete grade-level *quantity* of work, cut the quantity in half before you cut the difficulty. Demonstrate mastery with fewer items.

## PAGE 18 — TESTING ACCOMMODATIONS 📋

| Accommodation | What it solves | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Extended time (typically 1.5x; up to 2x with documentation) | Slower processing speed + need to re-read. | Standard on most 504/IEP plans. |
| Separate setting | Sensory + distraction load during tests. | Counselor's office, library, RTI room. |
| Frequent breaks during the test | Sustained attention fatigue. | One break per 30 min, 2–5 minutes. |
| Test administered in shorter sessions | Cognitive load over time. | Split a 60-min test across 2 days. |
| Test read aloud (non-reading tests) | Reading comprehension may be lower than content knowledge. | Common for non-ELA tests. |
| Use of scratch paper | Working memory support. | Always allow. |
| Manipulatives for math | Concrete supports for working memory. | Standard practice. |
| Reduced item count, same skill | Demonstrates mastery without volume. | More common at younger grades. |
| Visual cover sheet | Reduces visual overload of full test page. | Index card or blank paper to cover other items. |
| Calculator for computation | When the skill being tested is not computation. | Aligns to many state standards. |

⚠️ *Document everything.* If an accommodation is on the IEP/504, it must be provided on *every* test, including district benchmarks and state assessments. Skipping = legal exposure.

## PAGE 19 — SENSORY & MOVEMENT ACCOMMODATIONS 📋

ADHD brains seek movement and sensory input to *regulate*, not to disrupt.

| Accommodation | What it solves | How to do it |
|---|---|---|
| Fidget tool kit | Hand-busy = brain-attentive (for many ADHD kids). | Tactile, silent, doesn't break: putty, stress ball, textured chip clip. Bin available to the whole class. |
| Movement break menu | Pre-planned beats movement spikes during lessons. | 5 choices: water, bathroom, errand, stand, stretch. |
| Heavy work jobs | Proprioceptive input regulates the nervous system. | Carry books to the office, stack chairs, wipe boards. |
| Brain Gym / cross-body movement | Activates regulation pathways. | 90-second sequence between activities. |
| Bouncy band on desk legs | Foot-bouncing channel. | $8 elastic band stretched across front desk legs. |
| Chew necklace / gum (if permitted) | Oral sensory regulation. | Especially helpful for K–5 students. |
| Weighted lap pad | Deep-pressure calming. | Available on request; not assigned. |
| Designated "walk and talk" partner | Reduces seated demand. | For dialogue-style instruction. |
| 2-minute hallway walk | Reset escalating regulation. | Use *before* the meltdown, not after. |
| "Mission" errands | Combines movement with adult check-in. | Deliver an envelope to the office (which contains only a smiley face). |

**The fidget myth:** fidgets are not a reward, a privilege, or a distraction. They are *regulation tools*. Treat them like glasses or hearing aids.

## PAGE 21 — ACCOMMODATION SELECTION WORKSHEET 📋

## PAGE 23 — WHEN TO MOVE FROM ACCOMMODATION TO MODIFICATION 🧭

**An accommodation** changes *how* a student accesses grade-level material. The destination is unchanged.

**A modification** changes *what* the student is learning. The destination is different.

Most ADHD students need accommodations only. A small subset with co-occurring learning disabilities may need modifications.

| Sign you may need to escalate to a modification | What to do |
|---|---|
| Student is in Tier 3 accommodations and still failing at grade level. | Convene the IEP team. Consider modified curriculum in the highest-impact subject. |
| The cognitive gap exceeds what time + organization changes can close. | Request psychoeducational eval. |
| Student is in academic shutdown ("I'm stupid") for > 6 weeks. | Pause for socio-emotional support first; academic mods second. |
| The student passes tests but cannot produce work consistent with the grade. | Probably *not* a modification candidate — it's an output / EF issue. Stay with accommodations. |

**Do not modify when an accommodation could work.** Modification reduces access to grade-level content. It is appropriate when needed, harmful when it is used to avoid the harder work of accommodating.

⚖️ *Documentation matters.* Any modification must be in the IEP, agreed to by the team, and reviewed annually.

# SECTION 3 — BEHAVIOR TRACKING SYSTEM

## PAGE 24 — WHY TRACK? (AND WHY MOST TEACHERS TRACK THE WRONG THINGS) 🧭

**Most behavior tracking fails for one reason:** it documents the *behavior*, but never captures the *pattern*. You end up with a stack of incident reports and no insight.

Useful tracking answers three questions:

1. **What is happening?** (Define the behavior in observable terms.)
2. **When and where is it happening?** (Patterns by time of day, subject, peer, setting.)
3. **What function does it serve?** (Escape, attention, access, sensory regulation.)

If your tracking does not move you closer to answering #2 and #3, it is paperwork. Throw it out.

**The three best tools, ranked.**

| Tool | Best for | Time investment |
|---|---|---|
| ABC log (page 25) | Identifying triggers + function. | 2–3 min per incident, 5–10 incidents. |
| Frequency chart (page 26) | Counting how often a behavior happens. | 30 sec per occurrence, ongoing. |
| Heat map (page 27) | Spotting time/place patterns. | 5 min daily for a week. |

**The one rule of behavior tracking:**

> Track ONE behavior at a time, defined OBSERVABLY, for a defined PERIOD.

"Disruptive" is not a behavior. "Leaves seat without permission during independent work" is. The more observable the definition, the more useful the data.

## PAGE 32 — WHAT FOCUS ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE IN ADHD 🧭

You have been told "ADHD = can't focus." This is wrong, and it leads teachers to miss what is actually happening.

**The truth about ADHD attention:**

1. **It is dysregulated, not deficient.** ADHD students can focus — *intensely* — on what their dopamine system rewards. They cannot reliably focus on what it does not. This is why a student who can't sit through a math lesson can play Minecraft for four hours.
2. **It is interest-driven, not importance-driven.** Neurotypical brains can use *future* importance to drive present effort. ADHD brains use *present* interest or *immediate* consequence.
3. **It comes and goes minute-to-minute.** A 6-minute on-task stretch may be followed by a 3-minute drift. This is normal and expected. It is not "choice."
4. **"Off-task" is a continuum, not a switch.** Partial attention, internal distraction, hyperfocus on the wrong thing, and full mind-wandering all look similar from the outside.

**What this means for monitoring:**

- Stop measuring whether they are "paying attention." Start measuring *how long* they sustain it, *what helps*, and *what breaks* it.
- A 6-minute attention span is not a problem to punish. It is a baseline to build from.
- Hyperfocus is real. A student who locks in for 45 minutes on a topic of interest is not "faking" the ADHD — it is part of the disorder.

The tools in this section help you *see* the focus pattern instead of guessing at it.

# SECTION 5 — PARENT COMMUNICATION TOOLKIT

---

## PAGE 39 — THE PARENT COMMUNICATION MINDSET 🧭

Most parents of ADHD students have received one of three messages, over and over, since pre-K:

- "Your child is disruptive."
- "Your child won't try."
- "Have you considered…?" (a discipline tactic, a diet, a screen-time limit).

By the time they reach you, they are tired and they are braced. Their first read of your email is filtered through ten years of those messages. If your email looks like #4, you have lost them on the first sentence.

**The communication you want to build:**

| What parents experience as | What that creates |
|---|---|
| You see their child's strengths. | Openness. Partnership. |
| You are specific, not vague. | Trust. Action. |
| You are not asking them to fix it from home. | Relief. Reciprocity. |
| You bring solutions, not just problems. | Collaboration. |
| You communicate proactively, not only during crisis. | Stability. Long-term trust. |

**The two-to-one rule.**

For every concern you raise, send two positives first or in the same message. Not flattery — *specific* observations:

> "Olivia explained her thinking out loud to a peer today during math and helped her partner figure out the problem. I want you to know she's developing as a teacher of others, not just a student. **Now — separately — I want to share what we're working on:** finishing the written part of math when the verbal explanation feels done."

This pattern keeps the relationship alive over a long year.

## PAGE 42 — DIFFICULT CONVERSATION SCRIPTS 📎

When you have to share something hard. Use these as starting points, not word-for-word reads.

### Script 1 — "I'm noticing patterns that suggest an evaluation could help."

It tells us what [she/he] needs so we can give it to [her/him]. I'd love to talk about what next steps might look like. What questions do you have?

### Script 2 — "Today was a really hard day."

> Hi [parent], I'm calling because today was hard for [student]. I want you to know we kept [her/him] safe, [her/he] is okay now, and we're going to debrief tomorrow with the counselor. Here's what happened, briefly: [factual, observable account, 2–3 sentences]. We responded by: [calm de-escalation steps]. [She/he] needed [outcome — calm corner, check-in, etc.]. ### Script 3 — "I think we should hold off on a phone call until we're both calm."

> Thanks for reaching out. I can hear that you're frustrated, and honestly, I am too. I don't think either of us is going to be able to think clearly right now. Can I email you in the morning to set up a 20-minute call when we can both bring our best thinking? I want to get this right with [student] and that means we both need a clear head. Talk tomorrow.

### Script 4 — "The school recommends an evaluation. The parent has refused."

> I want to respect that this isn't the path you want. I also want to share what I'm seeing in class so we can keep talking about it. My job is to teach [student], and I'll keep doing that with the strategies I've described. I'm going to keep notes on what's working and what's not, and I'll share them with you regularly. ## PAGE 43 — STRENGTH-FIRST REPORTING FRAMEWORK 🧭

Every progress report, conference, and IEP meeting should follow this order:

1. **What this student does well.** Specific. Observable. Not generic.
2. **What is harder, and what I'm doing about it.** Observable behavior + your active strategy.
3. **What I'd love the family's help with.** Concrete, small, non-judgmental.
4. **What's next.** Clear plan + check-in date.

### Example — quarter progress note

> **What Marcus does well**
> Marcus is a strong oral reader and a generous classroom partner. He has helped two new students this quarter find their seats and supplies on their first day. When he is interested in a topic, he asks better follow-up questions than anyone in our literature circle.
>
> **Where we're working**
> Marcus is having a hard time finishing written work, especially after lunch. I've seen him start strong and then stall about 8 minutes in. We've added a wobble cushion, a privacy folder, and a 10-minute check-in. We're seeing progress on Tuesdays and Thursdays.
>
> **What would help from home**
> I'd love to learn whether Marcus seems to crash at home in the late afternoon too — and if so, what helps him reset. It would help me time his work demands at school.
>
> **What's next**
> I'll send a daily snapshot for the next two weeks so we can see the pattern together. Let's check back in on the 22nd by phone.

## PAGE 46 — WHEN (AND HOW) TO SUGGEST AN EVALUATION 🧭

When you suspect ADHD and the student is not yet diagnosed.

**Signs it's time to suggest an evaluation:**

- Behavior pattern is stable across 6+ weeks despite Tier 1–2 interventions.
- Academic performance is inconsistent — strong in some areas, severely impaired in others.
- Family reports similar patterns at home.
- The student is starting to describe themselves negatively ("I'm stupid," "I'm bad").

**How to phrase it without overstepping.**

You are *not* diagnosing. You are *not* recommending medication. You are sharing what you see and recommending a *path*.

> "I want to share something I'm noticing — and I want to be clear, I'm a teacher, not a clinician. What I see is [pattern]. That pattern has a lot of possible causes — and one way to know more is a comprehensive evaluation. The school can do an educational evaluation for support purposes, or you can pursue a clinical one through your pediatrician or a neuropsych. Either way, the point is to understand [student] better. The evaluation isn't a label — it's a map."

**What to give the parent.**

1. A specific list of *observable* behaviors (no interpretation).
2. A handout from CHADD, Understood, or your district's special education office.
3. A timeline: how to request a school evaluation (in writing), how long it takes, what comes next.
4. A reminder that an evaluation *does not* automatically lead to medication. Many ADHD plans involve only accommodations and behavioral supports.

**If the parent refuses.**

Document. Continue with classroom accommodations. Revisit gently every quarter. Most parents who initially refuse come around when they see consistent caring data over time.

## PAGE 47 — TIER 1 / TIER 2 / TIER 3 OVERVIEW (MTSS) 🧭

Most U.S. schools use a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) or Response to Intervention (RTI). The structure is roughly:

| Tier | Who | What | Documentation expected |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | All students | Universal classroom strategies; differentiated instruction. | Lesson plans; class-wide data. |
| **Tier 2** | ~10–15% of students | Targeted small-group intervention; supplemental support. | Intervention plan; progress monitoring every 1–2 weeks. |
| **Tier 3** | ~3–5% of students | Intensive individualized intervention. May lead to evaluation for IEP/504. | Detailed plan, weekly data, fidelity checks. |

**Where ADHD fits.**

- Most students with mild ADHD do well with **Tier 1** classroom accommodations (Section 2).
- Students who don't respond after 4–6 weeks of consistent Tier 1 should move to **Tier 2** — usually a small group focused on EF or behavior skills, plus targeted accommodations.
- Students who don't respond to Tier 2 after 6–8 weeks should move to **Tier 3** and be considered for formal evaluation.

**Why most ADHD interventions fail.**

Three reasons:

1. **Not done with fidelity.** The plan says "movement break every 30 min." The teacher does it Monday, forgets Tuesday, partly Wednesday. The data shows no improvement. *The intervention wasn't actually delivered.*
2. **Wrong intervention for the deficit.** Worksheets to "improve attention" don't help a kid whose problem is task initiation.
3. **Stopped too early.** A 2-week trial is not enough. Six weeks minimum to evaluate.

The forms in this section solve all three.

## PAGE 55 — WHAT A STUDENT SUCCESS PLAN IS 🧭

A Student Success Plan (SSP) is **not** an IEP. It is **not** a 504. It is a *living one-page profile* you build with the student — by 6th grade, ideally with the student — that captures:

- Who they are at their best.
- What gets in the way.
- What works.
- What's the plan.

**Why every ADHD student needs one:**

1. **It travels.** When the student gets a new teacher, you hand it over. They are not starting from scratch in September.
2. **It centers strengths.** Most ADHD students have never seen themselves described in writing in terms of what they're *good at*. This matters.
3. **It builds metacognition.** Helping the student name what helps them — and what doesn't — is half of the long-term work.
4. **It is shorter than an IEP.** A teacher can read it in 90 seconds.

**Who fills it out:**

| Grade | Who leads |
|---|---|
| K–2 | Teacher fills it out; parent reviews. |
| 3–5 | Teacher + student together; parent reviews. |
| 6+ | Student leads with teacher facilitation; parent reviews. |

The next pages walk you through building one.

# SECTION 8 — EMERGENCY DE-ESCALATION RESOURCES

> ⚠️ **A note before this section:** the resources in this section assume you are working with general classroom escalations — not violent or self-harm crises. Any situation involving danger to self or others requires immediate adult support, your school's crisis response protocol, and (when needed) emergency services. If a student is in active mental-health crisis, call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) or 911.

---

## PAGE 63 — THE 4 STAGES OF ESCALATION 🛟

Every meltdown follows a predictable arc. Knowing the stage tells you what to do — and what *not* to do.

| Stage | What it looks like | What is happening inside | What works |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Trigger / Anxiety** | Quiet shift. Less talking. Picks at things. Stares. Tone changes. | The student is sensing a demand or stressor they don't have capacity for. Cortisol rising. | **Notice.** Reduce demand. Soft check-in. Offer a break. This is where you have the most leverage. |
| **2. Defensive / Agitation** | Argues, refuses, says "no" loudly, slams items, postures. May insult or threaten verbally. | Frontal lobe going offline. Limbic system in charge. They are no longer thinking — they are reacting. | **Step back.** Lower voice. Reduce audience. Drop demand. Offer choice (not commands). Use the script on page 64. |
| **3. Crisis / Acting-out** | Throwing, hitting, eloping, screaming, self-harm. May be brief or extended. | Survival brain. Cannot process language well. Cannot make rational choices. | **Safety.** Clear the area. Call for help if needed. Do not chase. Use minimal words. Keep the student safe; keep the room safe. |
| **4. Recovery / Re-regulation** | Exhausted, tearful, withdrawn. Sometimes immediately fine — but burned out beneath. May feel shame. | Cortisol crash. Frontal lobe coming back online slowly. RSD often spikes here. | **Quiet presence.** No lecturing. Water. Rest. Reconnection. Debrief *later*, not now. Use the post-incident form on page 67. |

**The biggest mistake teachers make:** trying to *reason* during Stage 2 or 3. Reasoning, lecturing, or issuing consequences in those stages will escalate the crisis. The thinking brain is offline.

**Reason during Stage 1. Or during Stage 4 — much later. Never during 2 or 3.**

---

## PAGE 64 — DE-ESCALATION SCRIPTS 📎

🖨 *Print on a small index card. Keep in pocket or on lanyard.*

### Stage 1 — Trigger

> "Hey — you seem like something's on your mind. Want to take 2 minutes by the window? No pressure."

> "I noticed you got quiet. Want a job for a sec? Could you take this to the office for me?"

> "Want me to make this assignment shorter today? I noticed you started strong; let's get just the first half done."

### Stage 2 — Agitation

> *Quiet voice, no audience.* "I see this is hard. I'm not going to fight you on it. Let's take 5 minutes."

> "You don't have to answer me right now. Just go to the corner. I'll come check on you in a few."

> "I'm going to step away. When you're ready, give me a signal."

> ⚠️ *Do NOT say at this stage:* "Why are you doing this?" / "Stop right now." / "I'm not asking again." / "Look at me."

### Stage 3 — Crisis

> Use as few words as possible. Slow voice. Short phrases.
>
> "You're safe."
>
> "I'm here."
>
> "It's okay."
>
> Direct other students to a safe spot. Call your designated support adult. Do not physically engage unless your training and protocol allow it.

### Stage 4 — Recovery

> "I'm glad you came back."
>
> "We don't have to talk yet."
>
> "Want some water?"
>
> "When you're ready, I'll be over here. Take your time."

> *Hours or a day later, when calm:*
>
> "I want to check in about earlier. Not to lecture you. I want to figure out — together — what we could try next time. Can we look at this for 5 minutes?"

## PAGE 66 — CALM-DOWN SPACES IN THE CLASSROOM 🧭

Every classroom should have one. It is not punishment. It is regulation.

**Where to put it:**

- A back corner or behind a low bookshelf.
- Out of the main line of sight (sensory protection).
- Within the teacher's view (safety).

**What to put in it:**

| Item | Why |
|---|---|
| Bean bag or floor cushion | Body grounding. |
| Weighted lap pad or blanket | Deep pressure. |
| Picture book or magazine | Low-demand visual focus. |
| Fidget basket | Regulation, not entertainment. |
| Tissue box | Anchored shame-free presence. |
| Visual feelings chart | Naming the state. |
| Hourglass / sand timer | "When the sand stops, I'll come check on you." |
| A short "calm-down checklist" laminated card | Self-direction. |

**What NOT to put in:**

- A tablet or screen.
- A timer that counts down loudly.
- A clipboard for "reflection paragraphs" (this is punishment dressed as calm-down).
- A poster that says "Think about what you did."

**Rules of the calm corner:**

1. Any student can go without asking. Eye contact + walk-over.
2. The corner is not a consequence. Never assign it as punishment.
3. The teacher checks in after 3–5 minutes. Quiet, low-demand.
4. The student decides when to return. (Cap: when their body is calm.)

## PAGE 69 — EMERGENCY COMMUNICATION PLAN 🛟

What to do, in order, when a Stage 3 event happens.

**Before the year starts:**

1. Know your school's crisis protocol. Read it. Memorize the numbers.
2. Identify the *one adult* you call for support. Make sure they know.
3. Pre-write a parent contact message template (below). Save it as a draft.

**In the moment:**

1. **Safety first.** Move other students if needed. Do not physically engage unless trained and authorized.
2. **Call for help.** Use the signal — phone, intercom, hall monitor, classroom messenger.
3. **Stay calm. Stay near. Stay quiet.** Words make Stage 3 worse.
4. **Time-stamp.** Note the time the crisis began. Note the time it eased.

**Right after:**

1. Document on the post-incident form (page 67).
2. Call the parent — same day. Use the template below.
3. Notify admin in writing within 24 hours.
4. Talk to the counselor about next steps.

## PAGE 70 — WHAT DATA ACTUALLY HELPS (AND WHAT'S JUST PAPERWORK) 🧭

Schools love data. Many teachers come to hate it — because most of what they're asked to collect is *paperwork*, not data.

**Data is useful when:**

1. It is tied to a specific goal.
2. It is collected the same way each time.
3. It is reviewed and *used*.

**Data is paperwork when:**

1. It is collected and filed without being reviewed.
2. The metric doesn't connect to a decision rule.
3. It is collected differently by different adults.

**The four kinds of useful data:**

| Type | What it measures | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | How often a behavior occurs. | Discrete, countable behaviors (blurting, leaving seat). |
| **Duration** | How long a behavior or state lasts. | Engagement, time on task, tantrum length. |
| **Latency** | The delay between cue and response. | Task initiation, transition time. |
| **Interval** | Whether a behavior occurs during a defined window. | Whole-class observations, comparing across kids. |

The templates that follow cover all four — plus rating scales and a one-page dashboard for IEP meetings.

⚠️ *Rule:* every form you collect should answer the question, *"What decision will this help me make?"* If you can't answer that, don't collect it.

# SECTION 10 — TEACHER SELF-CARE AND BURNOUT PREVENTION

> This section is for you. Not "you, the teacher who manages everyone else." Just you. Read it on a Sunday afternoon with a cup of something warm.

## PAGE 78 — THE TEACHER BURNOUT REALITY 🧭

Teaching students with ADHD is one of the most cognitively demanding jobs in a school. You are doing:

- Direct instruction.
- Real-time behavior management.
- Emotional regulation (theirs *and* yours).
- Documentation.
- Family communication.
- Differentiation across 20+ unique brains.

In a typical hour, you make somewhere between **600 and 1,500 micro-decisions.** That is more than air traffic controllers. There is no equivalent profession that asks for this many decisions for this little time to recover.

**Burnout isn't weakness. It is depletion.**

The three signs you are heading there:

1. **Emotional exhaustion.** You are running on fumes by Wednesday.
2. **Depersonalization.** You start describing your students with terms you would have been horrified by in your first year ("That kid…").
3. **Reduced sense of accomplishment.** Even your wins start feeling small.

If any of these is true for you right now, this section is not optional. This section is your job today.

> "You cannot pour from an empty cup. You also cannot pour while you are *being* the cup, while *holding* the cup, while *filling everyone else's* cup. So put the cup down."
> — *Anonymous teacher, paraphrased*

---

## PAGE 79 — THE DAILY DECOMPRESSION ROUTINE 📎

The transition from school to "off" is the most underrated self-care tool teachers ignore.

**The 15-minute decompression protocol:**

```
MINUTE 0–3:   Physical reset
              Stretch arms over head. 5 deep breaths.
              Drink a full glass of water before leaving the building.

MINUTE 3–7:   Brain dump (paper or voice memo, never to anyone)
              - What was hard today?
              - What worked?
              - What's tomorrow's first thing?
              Put the brain dump in a drawer. Done.

MINUTE 7–12:  Boundary cross (literal)
              Walk to your car the long way.
              At a specific landmark (a tree, a sign, an intersection),
              say out loud or in your head:
                "School is back there. I am here now."

MINUTE 12–15: Body restoration
              Move (walk, dance, stretch). Eat something with protein.
              Do not check email until the next morning.
```

🧠 *Why this works:* the nervous system holds the day until you give it permission to release. The brain-dump → physical movement → boundary cross sequence tells your nervous system: *the day is over.*

**The two-week test.** Do this routine — exactly — for 10 school days. Then evaluate. Most teachers report falling asleep faster, dreading Mondays less, and feeling less reactive at home.

## PAGE 80 — BOUNDARY SCRIPTS 📎

You are allowed to have limits. These scripts protect them without burning bridges.

### Email after hours

> Hi [parent name], I got your email. I'll respond on [day]. I keep my evenings for my family, and I find I respond more thoughtfully when I'm not running on fumes. I'll have a real answer for you by [day]. Thanks for your patience.

### Lunch interruption

> I'm here. Tell me one sentence — is it urgent for *this hour*? If not, can we talk at 3:15? My brain is at lunch right now.

### "Quick favor" requests

> I want to say yes, and I know I'll do it badly if I do. I have to say no to this one. Is there another way I can help?

### Admin asks for one more thing

> I can do one of: [X] / [Y] / [Z]. I can't do all three before Friday. Which is the priority?

### A colleague is venting endlessly

> I'm in it too — I get it. I have 20 minutes of capacity for this today. Let's use that, and then I have to go take care of myself.

### A parent is yelling

> I can hear you're upset. I want to be in this conversation with you. I need us to lower the volume so I can give you my best attention. Can we take a breath?

### Your own inner critic

> If a friend had today, I would not say to her what I am saying to me.

🖨 *Print on a sticky note. Stick on your laptop edge.*

## PAGE 81 — VICARIOUS TRAUMA AWARENESS 🧭

Teachers of students with ADHD, trauma histories, or behavior challenges are at high risk of **vicarious trauma** — the cumulative emotional load of caring for people in distress.

**Signs it may be happening to you:**

- You are having intrusive thoughts about a student outside of school.
- You are losing sleep over specific kids.
- You feel emotionally numb or flat at home.
- You feel guilty for taking time off.
- You overidentify with a student's pain.
- You're crying in your car. Not occasionally — regularly.

**This is not a moral failure.** This is what happens to people whose nervous systems hold other people's distress every day, with no formal protocol for unloading it.

**What helps (research-backed):**

1. **Talk to someone trained in vicarious trauma.** A school counselor, a therapist, an employee assistance program — not your spouse, not your colleague.
2. **Have a low-stakes hobby that uses your hands.** Cooking, gardening, knitting, building. Bilateral movement helps process emotional load.
3. **Limit consumption of bad news.** Your nervous system is already at capacity.
4. **Get outside. Daily. Not negotiable.**
5. **Protect your sleep.** Insomnia is often the first sign. A two-week sleep dip is your alarm system — listen.

> *"You will lose yourself in this job if you do not protect yourself from this job."*
> — *Veteran SPED teacher, 28 years in*

## PAGE 83 — 5-MINUTE RESET STRATEGIES 📎

For when you have 5 minutes between blocks and you can feel yourself fraying.

| Strategy | What to do | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **5-4-3-2-1 grounding** | Name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. | Pulls cortex back online. |
| **Cold water on wrists** | Run cold water on inside of wrists for 30 sec. | Activates vagus nerve. |
| **Box breathing** | 4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold. Repeat 4 times. | Resets nervous system. |
| **Stretch + sigh** | Reach arms overhead, exhale loudly. 3 times. | Releases trapezius tension. |
| **Step outside** | Any door, any sky, even 60 seconds. | Light + outdoor air drop cortisol. |
| **Photo from outside school** | A pet, a kid, a beach trip. Look for 30 sec. | Re-orients to identity beyond teacher. |
| **Tea / coffee ritual** | The making, not just the drinking. | Sensory grounding + small accomplishment. |
| **Hand on heart** | Literally place hand on chest. 1 minute. | Self-soothing touch — works even from yourself. |
| **Voice memo** | 90 seconds, talk it out to no one. Delete. | Externalizes emotional load. |
| **Movement** | 30 jumping jacks, 30 wall pushups, or a walk to the printer. | Shakes off the cortisol. |

📌 *Pick two. Practice them in non-crisis times so they are automatic when you need them.*

## PAGE 85 — WHEN TO GET HELP — RESOURCES AND HOTLINES 🛟

The signs you need more than a self-care page:

- You are crying daily.
- You are using alcohol, food, or screens to numb at the end of every day.
- You are thinking about quitting and have been for more than a month.
- You can no longer feel pride in any of your work.
- You are losing time — driving home and not remembering the drive.
- You are having thoughts of hurting yourself.
- You are dreading specific students with an intensity that frightens you.

**This is not weakness.** This is your nervous system asking for help.

**Where to go.**

## PAGE 87 — GLOSSARY OF ADHD / SPECIAL EDUCATION TERMS 🧭

| Term | Plain-English meaning |
|---|---|
| **504 Plan** | A federal civil-rights plan that requires reasonable accommodations for students with disabilities. Does not require specialized instruction. |
| **ABC (Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence)** | A method of analyzing behavior by recording what happened before, during, and after each incident. |
| **Accommodation** | A change in *how* a student accesses learning. Destination unchanged. |
| **ADD vs. ADHD** | Old terminology. ADHD is the only current diagnosis (with three presentations). |
| **ADHD-PI / ADHD-PH / ADHD-C** | Predominantly Inattentive / Predominantly Hyperactive / Combined types. |
| **BIP (Behavior Intervention Plan)** | A written plan addressing target behaviors and their function, with replacement strategies. |
| **CBT** | Cognitive Behavioral Therapy. Often used for ADHD + co-occurring anxiety. |
| **DMN (Default Mode Network)** | The brain network active during mind-wandering. Stays "on" in ADHD even when focused. |
| **Executive Function (EF)** | The set of cognitive skills that govern self-management: working memory, inhibition, planning, etc. |
| **FBA (Functional Behavior Assessment)** | The formal evaluation of *why* a behavior is happening. Precedes a BIP. |
| **Fidelity** | The degree to which an intervention is delivered as designed. |
| **Hyperfocus** | The ADHD ability to lock into a highly interesting task for extended time. Real, but not voluntary. |
| **IDEA** | Individuals with Disabilities Education Act — the federal law governing IEPs. |
| **IEP (Individualized Education Program)** | A legal document for students who qualify under IDEA for specialized instruction. |
| **Latency** | The delay between a cue and a response — a measure of task initiation. |
| **MTSS** | Multi-Tiered System of Supports. Same idea as RTI; many districts use both terms. |
| **Modification** | A change in *what* a student is learning. Destination changed. |
| **ODD (Oppositional Defiant Disorder)** | A persistent pattern of defiance, often co-occurring with ADHD. Often a symptom of unmet ADHD needs. |
| **PFC (Prefrontal Cortex)** | The brain region for executive function. Develops 2–3 years late in ADHD. |
| **RSD (Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria)** | Intense emotional response to perceived rejection or criticism. Common in ADHD. |
| **RTI (Response to Intervention)** | The tiered support framework for academic and behavioral interventions. |
| **SDI (Specially Designed Instruction)** | Instruction adapted in content, methodology, or delivery — a defining feature of IEPs. |
| **SEL (Social-Emotional Learning)** | Curriculum and practices that support emotional and social skills. |
| **SPED (Special Education)** | Shorthand for the special education system / staff. |
| **Tier 1 / 2 / 3** | Levels of intervention intensity under MTSS / RTI. |
| **Time blindness** | Difficulty perceiving the passage of time. Common in ADHD. |
| **Twice-exceptional (2e)** | A student who is both gifted and has a disability (e.g., gifted + ADHD). |
| **Working memory** | The mental "scratch pad" that holds info while you use it. Frequently impaired in ADHD. |
