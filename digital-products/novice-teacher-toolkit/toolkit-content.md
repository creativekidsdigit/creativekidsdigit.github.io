# The Novice Teacher's Lesson Planning Toolkit

*A practical, printable system for new teachers. Beginning, middle, and end.*

---

## A note before you start

Teaching is hard. The first year is the hardest, and the second isn't a holiday either.
This toolkit is not theory. It will not tell you about Vygotsky. It will tell you
what to write on the board on Monday, what to do when the lesson collapses on
Wednesday, and what to put on the photocopier on Friday.

Every page answers one question a new teacher actually asks. Print the pages
marked **🖨 PRINT** and keep a stack in your drawer. Print the pages marked
**📎 KEEP HANDY** and tape them inside your planner.

You don't have to read this in order. Use the table of contents and grab what
you need today.

## How to use this toolkit

**Week 1:** Read Section 1. Use the template for every lesson, even if it feels slow. It will not feel slow by Friday.

**Week 2:** Add Section 2. Time-box every block of your lesson. You will run over. That's fine.

**Week 3:** Pick 3 activities from Section 3. Use one per day. Don't try all 10 in week one.

**Week 4:** Add Section 4. Pick one assessment method. Use it every day for a week.

**Month 2:** Use Section 5 every Sunday. 30 minutes. No exceptions.

**Always:** Section 6 is in your bottom drawer. You will need it. That's not a failure — that's teaching.

# Section 1 — The Reusable Lesson Plan Template

## Why this template is short

Most lesson plan templates new teachers are given have 30+ fields. They are not built for new teachers. They are built for inspectors.

This template has 8 fields. You can fill it in 10 minutes. You will use it every day for the rest of your career.

---

## The 8 fields, explained

### 1. Date / Class / Topic

One line. *"Tuesday Oct 14 — 2AS-B — Reported Speech (introduction)."*

If you can't summarize the topic in 4 words, the lesson is too big. Cut it.

### 2. Lesson objective (the one sentence test)

Finish this sentence in writing: **"By the end of this lesson, students will be able to ____."**

The blank must be a verb you can see students do: *write, list, identify, compare, explain, build, perform, draw, produce, recall, choose.*

The blank must NOT be: *understand, know, learn, appreciate, be aware of.* You cannot see understanding. You can only see what students do.

**Good:**
> By the end of this lesson, students will be able to **transform 5 direct-speech sentences into reported speech.**

**Bad:**
> Students will understand reported speech.

If you can't write the good version, you don't yet know what you're teaching. Don't go to class.

### 3. Materials needed

Bullet list. Be specific.
- 30 photocopies of Worksheet 4
- Whiteboard markers (red + blue)
- Phone or tablet (for the audio clip)
- Index cards (5 per group of 4 = 40 cards)

If anything on this list isn't ready by 7:30am, that thing is your job at 7:31am.

### 4. The hook (3 minutes)

How will you grab attention in the first 3 minutes? One specific idea.
- A short video (under 60 seconds)
- A surprising statistic on the board
- A physical demo
- A question with a controversial answer
- A photo with no caption ("What is happening here? Why?")

The hook is not "write the date and the title." That's not a hook. That's homework for you.

### 5. The main activity (25 minutes)

What students will *do*. Not what you will say.

Use the **I do / We do / You do** structure:
- **I do (5 min):** Show one example. Explain your thinking out loud.
- **We do (10 min):** Do a second example together. They tell you what to do.
- **You do (10 min):** They do 3 examples on their own (or in pairs).

If you can't fit this in 25 minutes, the activity is too big. Cut it.

### 6. The check for understanding (5 minutes)

How will you know if they got it? Pick ONE:
- Exit ticket (one question on a sticky note)
- Whiteboard show-me (everyone holds up an answer)
- Thumbs up / sideways / down on a self-rated confidence scale
- One-word summary from each table

**This is the most-skipped step by new teachers.** Do not skip it. If you skip it, you don't actually know if they learned anything.

### 7. The wrap-up (5 minutes)

- 1 minute: "What's one thing you learned today?" (call on 3 students)
- 1 minute: Preview tomorrow ("Tomorrow we'll use this to do X")
- 3 minutes: Pack up, hand back materials, line up

Do not let students leave loud. The wrap-up is what they will remember.

### 8. Reflection (write this AFTER the lesson)

3 lines. Right after the lesson, before you forget.
- What worked: ____
- What didn't: ____
- Tomorrow's adjustment: ____

You will be a better teacher in March because of what you wrote in October.

# Section 2 — The 45-Minute Class Structure

## The 10 / 25 / 10 split

| Block | Time | Purpose | Energy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Beginning** | 10 min | Land them, set the goal | High but contained |
| **Middle** | 25 min | The actual learning | Building, focused |
| **End** | 10 min | Lock it in, leave on time | Calm, confident |

This is not optional. New teachers fail by either:
- Spending 20 minutes on the warm-up because students seem engaged → no time for actual learning
- Skipping the wrap-up because the lesson ran long → students leave confused and the bell rings on chaos

A bell-rings-on-chaos lesson is the one that haunts you at 8pm.

---

## The Beginning (10 minutes)

| Min | Activity | What you do | What students do |
|----|---|---|---|
| 0–2 | **Settling** | Greet at the door. One-line hello to each student. | Sit down. Get out materials. |
| 2–5 | **Hook** | The 3-minute hook from your plan | Watch / read / react |
| 5–7 | **Objective on the board** | Write the "students will be able to" sentence. Read it out loud. | Copy it into their notebook. |
| 7–10 | **Connect to prior learning** | "Last lesson we did X. Today we'll use it to do Y." | Pair-share one thing they remember about X. |

**Why this order matters:** the hook gets them in the room mentally. The objective tells them what success looks like. The prior-learning bridge tells them this is not a random lesson — it's part of a story.

---

## The Middle (25 minutes)

This is where the **I do / We do / You do** lives.

### I do (5 minutes)
You demonstrate. You think out loud. Students watch.

> "Watch me. I'm going to take this direct-speech sentence — *She said: 'I am tired.'* — and turn it into reported speech. First I look at the verb tense in the quote. *Am* is present. So when I report it later, I shift to past. *She said she was tired.* See how *am* became *was*? Let me do another one..."

Common mistake: new teachers turn this into a 15-minute monologue. Cap it at 5. Set a timer.

### We do (10 minutes)
Same problem type, but students tell you what to do next. You write what they say on the board.

> "OK, your turn to drive. Here's a new sentence: *He said: 'I will call you.'* What's the first thing I do?"

If they can't tell you what to do, you went too fast in the I-do. Back up. That's not failure; that's teaching.

### You do (10 minutes)
Students work alone or in pairs. You walk the room. You do not return to your desk.

What you're looking for as you walk:
- 3 students who are stuck → quick whisper-help
- 3 students who are flying → give them a harder version
- The middle 20 → one or two corrections, lots of "good, keep going"

---

## The End (10 minutes)

| Min | Activity | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| 35–37 | **Quick recap** | "Tell your shoulder partner: what's the rule for shifting tense in reported speech?" Then call on 2 pairs. |
| 37–40 | **Exit ticket** | One question on a sticky note. They drop it in a box on the way out. |
| 40–43 | **Preview tomorrow** | "Tomorrow we'll use this to write a news report about something in the school." |
| 43–45 | **Pack up + line up** | Music optional. Calm, not loud. |

The bell does not dismiss your class. **You** dismiss your class.

---

## 🖨 PRINT — Worked example: 45-minute English lesson

> **Class:** 2AS-B  ·  **Topic:** Reported Speech intro  ·  **Time:** 9:00–9:45

| Min | Block | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0–2 | Settling | Greet at door, "How was the match yesterday?" |
| 2–5 | Hook | Show 30s clip: news anchor reporting what president said. Question on board: *"Why didn't they say it the same way?"* |
| 5–7 | Objective | Write: *"By the end you will rewrite 5 direct-speech sentences as reported speech."* |
| 7–10 | Bridge | "Last lesson we did past simple. Today we use it to report what someone said yesterday." |
| 10–15 | I do | Demo on board: *She said "I am tired."* → *She said she was tired.* Think aloud: tense shift. |
| 15–25 | We do | New example. Students dictate the steps. Write on board. Two more examples. |
| 25–35 | You do | Worksheet 4: 5 sentences. Pairs. I walk and check. |
| 35–37 | Recap | Shoulder-partner: "What rule did we use?" |
| 37–40 | Exit ticket | Sticky note: *"Rewrite one sentence: 'They said: We are happy.'"* |
| 40–43 | Preview | "Tomorrow you'll write a 5-sentence news report." |
| 43–45 | Pack up | Calm. Bell. Dismiss. |

# Section 3 — 10 Activities You Can Use Tomorrow

For each activity:
- **What:** one sentence
- **When to use it:** the moment in the lesson
- **Setup time:** 0–60 seconds, ideally
- **Time the activity takes**
- **Materials**
- **Common mistake new teachers make**
- **One concrete example** (English class, but the structure works for any subject)

---

## 1. Think-Pair-Share

**What:** Students think alone, then talk to a partner, then share with the class.

**When to use:** Any time you ask a question that more than 3 students should answer.

**Setup:** 0 seconds. You just say it.

**Time:** 4 minutes total (1 think, 2 pair, 1 share).

**Materials:** None.

**Common mistake:** Skipping the silent think. Without it, only the loud students think; the others copy.

**Example:** *"Think for 60 seconds: what's the difference between **few** and **a few**? (Pause. Wait. Hold the silence.) Now turn to your partner and share. (2 min.) Pair 4, what did you decide?"*

---

## 2. Quick Write

**What:** A 3-minute timed writing on a single prompt. No erasing.

**When to use:** As a hook OR as a check for understanding.

**Setup:** 30 seconds. Write the prompt. Set the timer.

**Time:** 3–5 minutes.

**Materials:** A prompt. A timer.

**Common mistake:** Reading them all out loud. You don't need to. Skim 5–6 while students work; that's your formative data.

**Example prompts:**
- *"Describe yesterday in 5 sentences using the past simple."*
- *"What is one thing you don't yet understand about reported speech?"*
- *"Convince me, in 3 sentences, that homework should be optional."*

---

## 3. Gallery Walk

**What:** Student work goes on the walls; students rotate and leave sticky-note feedback.

**When to use:** After group work or after a writing task you want students to see each other's versions of.

**Setup:** 2 minutes. Tape student work to walls. Hand out sticky notes.

**Time:** 10–12 minutes.

**Materials:** Sticky notes, tape, student work.

**Common mistake:** Letting it become a chat. Give a feedback prompt: *"On each piece, write ONE thing you'd steal for your own work."* Movement + writing = no chat.

**Example:** Students wrote 5-sentence opinion paragraphs. Tape them up. Each student visits 4 paragraphs and leaves one sticky each.

---

## 4. Exit Ticket

**What:** One question, answered on a small piece of paper, dropped in a box on the way out.

**When to use:** Last 3 minutes of every lesson, ideally.

**Setup:** 10 seconds. Write the question on the board.

**Time:** 3 minutes.

**Materials:** Paper scraps, a box.

**Common mistake:** Asking *"Did you understand?"* Useless — they all say yes. Ask them to **do** the thing.

**Good exit-ticket questions:**
- *Rewrite this sentence in reported speech: "He said: 'I will come.'"*
- *Give one example of a present-perfect verb from your own life.*
- *In one sentence, what was today's lesson about?*
- *What's one question you still have?*

---

## 5. Four Corners

**What:** Four signs in the room: *Strongly Agree / Agree / Disagree / Strongly Disagree.* Read a statement; students walk to the corner that matches.

**When to use:** Opinion-based topics, debates, opening discussions.

**Setup:** 1 minute. Tape four signs in four corners.

**Time:** 8–12 minutes (3–4 statements).

**Materials:** 4 printed signs. Statements you've prepared.

**Common mistake:** Using factual statements. *"The capital of France is Paris"* is not a 4-corners question. Use opinions.

**Example statements (English class):**
- *Homework should be banned in secondary school.*
- *Watching films in English helps more than reading books.*
- *Texting is ruining the language.*

After each statement: ask 1–2 students from each corner to defend their position. Don't argue with them. Just listen.

---

## 6. Concept Map

**What:** A visual diagram of how ideas connect. Central concept in the middle; branches outward.

**When to use:** End of a unit; or as a study tool before a test.

**Setup:** 30 seconds. Show one example on the board.

**Time:** 15 minutes (alone or in pairs).

**Materials:** Blank paper, pens.

**Common mistake:** Letting it become a list. A list is not a concept map. Force them to draw lines that show connections, and label the lines.

**Example:** Central concept: *"Tenses."* Branches: present simple, past simple, present continuous, etc. Lines between them labeled with rules: *"adds -ed," "adds -ing," "uses 'have/has'."*

---

## 7. Numbered Heads Together

**What:** Groups of 4. Each member numbered 1–4. You ask a question. The group discusses. Then you call a number — that person from each group answers.

**When to use:** Review, practice, any time you'd otherwise call on volunteers.

**Setup:** 1 minute (assign numbers).

**Time:** 3–4 minutes per question.

**Materials:** None. Optional: small whiteboards for each group to write on.

**Common mistake:** Calling the same number twice in a row. They figure it out and the quiet ones stop preparing. Vary it.

**Example:** Question: *"What's the difference between past simple and present perfect?"* Groups discuss for 2 minutes. *"Number 3, please answer."* Number 3 from each of the 7 groups answers in turn — 30 seconds each.

---

## 8. Jigsaw

**What:** Each student in a group becomes the "expert" on one piece of the content, then teaches it to their group.

**When to use:** When you have 4 sub-topics that don't depend on each other.

**Setup:** 5 minutes (you need 4 short texts, one per sub-topic).

**Time:** 25 minutes total. (10 expert, 12 teaching, 3 wrap.)

**Materials:** 4 short texts (~200 words each).

**Common mistake:** Not giving the expert groups time. They need 10 full minutes to actually understand their bit. If you give them 4, they teach garbage.

**Example:** Topic: *"Four ways to use 'have' in English."* Four expert groups: have as possession, have as auxiliary (have done), have to (obligation), have got. Each becomes expert. Then mixed back into home groups of 4 (one expert from each topic). Each teaches their bit for 3 minutes.

---

## 9. One-Word Check-In

**What:** Each student says a single word. About anything you ask.

**When to use:** Start of class (mood check) or end (learning check).

**Setup:** 0 seconds.

**Time:** 90 seconds for 30 students.

**Materials:** None.

**Common mistake:** Letting students explain. Stop them. The point is one word. Speed matters.

**Example prompts:**
- *"One word: how are you arriving today?"* (mood)
- *"One word: today's lesson."* (recall)
- *"One word: what made today hard?"* (reflection)

You learn an enormous amount from this. *"Tired. Tired. Confused. Tired. Hungry. OK. Tired."* That's a class that needs a snack and a movement break, not a quiz.

---

## 10. Whiteboard Show-Me

**What:** Every student has a mini-whiteboard. You ask a question. They write the answer. On 3, everyone holds up.

**When to use:** Any time you want a quick read on the whole class.

**Setup:** 30 seconds (hand out boards) — or zero, if you keep them under desks.

**Time:** 2 minutes per question.

**Materials:** Mini-whiteboards (or laminated paper / blank A5 sheets in plastic sleeves), dry-erase markers.

**Common mistake:** Letting students show their answer before you say "show me." That tells the others. No early reveals.

**Example:** *"On your whiteboards, rewrite this: 'They said we are happy.' (Pause 30 seconds.) ... 1 ... 2 ... 3, show me!"* Look around. You will see 30 answers in 5 seconds. The 6 who got it wrong are your next 5 minutes of small-group help.

# Section 4 — Simple Assessment Methods for Beginners

## The 3 types, in 3 lines

- **Diagnostic:** Where are they NOW, before I teach? (Done in week one of a unit.)
- **Formative:** Are they getting it AS I teach? (Done every lesson.)
- **Summative:** Did they get it BY THE END? (Done at the end of a unit.)

New teachers over-rotate on summative (the big test) and under-rotate on formative (the daily check). This is backwards. The big test only tells you what's already too late. The daily check tells you what to teach tomorrow.

---

## 7 quick assessment methods

For each: how to do it, what to do with the data, time required.

### 1. Thumbs Up / Sideways / Down

**How:** *"Thumbs up if you fully get it. Sideways if you sort of get it. Down if you're lost."*

**What you do with it:** If most are sideways, do one more example together. If most are down, you've gone too far — back up.

**Time:** 10 seconds.

**Note:** Some students will lie. That's fine — the patterns still tell you something. If 6 hands are down, those 6 need help.

---

### 2. Exit Ticket (one question)

**How:** Write one question on the board in the last 3 minutes. Students write the answer on a small piece of paper, drop it in a box on the way out.

**What you do with it:** Spend 5 minutes after class skimming. Sort into 3 piles: got it / partial / lost. The "lost" pile is who you re-teach tomorrow morning.

**Time:** 3 min in class, 5 min after.

---

### 3. 3-2-1

**How:** At the end of class, students write: 3 things they learned, 2 questions they have, 1 thing they'll use.

**What you do with it:** Tomorrow's hook is one of their questions, answered.

**Time:** 5 min.

---

### 4. Single-Point Rubric

**How:** Standard rubrics list 4 levels (excellent / good / fair / weak). For new teachers, write only the "good" column. Then on each student's work, mark *Above / Meets / Below* the standard.

**What you do with it:** You spend 90 seconds per paper instead of 5 minutes. Faster feedback. Better feedback.

**Time:** 90 sec/paper.

**Example:**

> **Standard for a 5-sentence opinion paragraph:**
> - States an opinion in the first sentence
> - Gives at least 2 reasons
> - Uses linking words (because, also, however)
> - Ends with a conclusion
> - 5+ complete sentences with correct punctuation
>
> *Above the standard: ____*
> *Meets the standard: ____*
> *Below the standard: ____*

---

### 5. Two Stars and a Wish

**How:** Peer or self-assessment. Each student finds 2 things to praise (stars) and 1 thing to improve (wish) on a piece of work.

**What you do with it:** Use it for revision, not grading. Students revise based on feedback before you grade.

**Time:** 5 min.

---

### 6. Stop Light

**How:** Three coloured cards (or just three columns on the board). Each student puts their initial on red (lost), yellow (almost), or green (got it).

**What you do with it:** Group by colour for the next activity. Greens get harder problems. Reds get a re-explanation.

**Time:** 2 min.

---

### 7. The 5-Question Quick Quiz

**How:** End of class, 5 multiple-choice or short-answer questions. Students self-check or swap and check.

**What you do with it:** *Don't grade these.* Use them as a learning tool. Students who got 2/5 know to re-study.

**Time:** 8 min.

---

## What to do with the data (the part novices skip)

You collected the data. Now what?

**Three rules.**

1. **One adjustment per lesson, not five.** If three students didn't get it, plan one re-teach moment for tomorrow. Don't redesign the whole unit.

2. **Re-teach at the start of the next lesson, not the end.** "Yesterday's exit tickets showed me half of you struggled with X. Let's do one example together." Three minutes. Then move on.

3. **Stop collecting data you don't use.** If you do exit tickets every day but never read them, stop. Do them twice a week and read them.

# Section 5 — The Weekly Planning Sheet System

## The 30-minute Sunday ritual

Every Sunday. 30 minutes. Same time. Same place. Same coffee.

This is not optional. The Sunday 30 saves 5 hours during the week.

| Min | What you do |
|---|---|
| 0–5 | Look at last week's reflection notes. What didn't work? What carries over? |
| 5–10 | Write each lesson's **objective** for the week. Just the sentence — *"By the end of Tuesday, students will be able to ____."* |
| 10–20 | Sketch the **main activity** for each day. One line each. Just the verb-noun: *"Worksheet 4 in pairs," "Gallery walk," "Quick write."* |
| 20–25 | Make the **photocopy list** for the week. One trip to the photocopier on Monday morning, not five. |
| 25–30 | Write **one progressive thread** that connects Mon → Fri. *"Monday introduces; Tuesday practices; Wednesday extends; Thursday reviews; Friday assesses."* |

You do not write full lesson plans on Sunday. You sketch the week. The full plan happens the night before each lesson — and because the sketch is done, the night-before plan is 10 minutes, not 60.

## How the progressive thread works

A week of lessons should not be 5 unrelated lessons. It should be a 5-day arc.

**Bad week (no thread):**
- Mon: present simple
- Tue: photo description
- Wed: a song
- Thu: present continuous
- Fri: a quiz

**Good week (clear thread — *"By Friday, students can describe a moment in time."*):**
- Mon: present simple (introduce — what is happening generally)
- Tue: present continuous (introduce — what is happening RIGHT NOW)
- Wed: contrast the two (when do we use which?)
- Thu: photo description practice (use both tenses to describe a photo)
- Fri: 5-minute speaking task — describe a photo in pairs (assessment)

Same content. Completely different week. The students feel like they're going somewhere because they are.

# Section 6 — The Teacher Emergency Kit

When the lesson dies. When tech fails. When you go home crying. This section is what you do.

Keep this in your bottom drawer. Tape a copy inside the cupboard door. You will need it.

---

## When the lesson finishes 10 minutes early

You planned 45 minutes. You're done at 35. Now what.

Five 5-minute fillers, every one of which is **actually instructional**, not babysitting:

### Filler 1 — Two Truths and a Lie (in the target language)
Each student writes 3 sentences about themselves: 2 true, 1 false. Read aloud; class guesses the lie. Practices sentence structure, listening, fluency. **5 min.**

### Filler 2 — The 60-Second Speech
You give a topic. They get 60 seconds to think. Then 3 random students stand and speak for 60 seconds on the topic. *No notes.* **5 min.**

### Filler 3 — Word Chain
You say a word. Next student says a word that starts with the last letter of yours. (English: *bread → dance → end → door...*) When someone gets stuck, restart. **5 min.**

### Filler 4 — Categories
*"In 60 seconds, write as many ___ as you can."* (Things in a kitchen. Verbs of motion. Adjectives that start with C.) Then share top answers. **5 min.**

### Filler 5 — Question of the Day
Write one open question on the board. *"What's one thing you'd change about this school if you were the head?"* Pairs discuss for 2 min. Volunteers share. **5 min.**

These are not time-killers. They build fluency, vocabulary, confidence. They will become some of your favourite lessons.

---

## When tech / materials fail

The projector won't work. Your USB is missing. The photocopier ate Worksheet 4. You have nothing.

Five no-prep 20-minute lessons that work for any subject:

### No-prep lesson 1 — The 5-Sentence Story

Give a topic (anything: yesterday, my grandmother, a memory of school). They write a 5-sentence story. Then 4 students read theirs out loud. Then peer feedback (Two Stars and a Wish). **20 min.**

### No-prep lesson 2 — The Newspaper

Pretend the front page of tomorrow's newspaper. Students write headlines about: an event in their life, an event at school, an event in the country. **20 min.**

### No-prep lesson 3 — Reverse Engineering

Write a sentence on the board with one word missing. Students brainstorm 5 possible words that could fit. Discuss why some work and some don't. (Teaches grammar without you teaching grammar.) **20 min.**

### No-prep lesson 4 — Vocabulary Mapping

Pick a word from yesterday's lesson. Brainstorm: synonyms, antonyms, words it sounds like, words you'd use it with. On the board, build a giant map together. **20 min.**

### No-prep lesson 5 — The Interview

Pairs. Each interviews the other for 5 minutes about a chosen topic (their week, their family, their favourite anything). Then writes 5 sentences about their partner. Practices listening + writing + person-shifting (he/she/they). **20 min.**

## When you're losing the room

The energy crashes. They're chatting. They're on phones. The lesson is alive but the students are gone.

### The 90-second reset

1. **Stop talking.** Mid-sentence. Don't say "Can I have your attention." Just stop.
2. **Wait.** They will notice the silence. It will take 10–20 seconds. Hold it.
3. **One physical instruction.** *"Stand up."* Or *"Move one seat to the left."* Or *"Take out a piece of paper."*
4. **One 30-second task.** *"Write your name and one thing you want to learn today."*
5. **Restart.** Different energy. New angle.

Why this works: a content reset is invisible to them. A *physical* reset breaks the trance.

### The "stand up and switch chairs" trick

When a discussion has died and you can't restart it: *"Everyone stand. Move to a chair on the other side of the room. Pair with whoever is next to you."* New partner = new ideas.

### The 1-question circle

Push the desks back. Sit in a circle on the floor or on chairs. One question. Everyone answers in turn. You go last. *"What's one thing that would make this class better?"* Listen. Take notes.

You will get more honest data in 10 minutes of this than 10 weeks of surveys.

## Behavior emergency scripts

You will get into situations where you don't know what to say. Memorize these three scripts.

### Script 1 — A student is on their phone

**Don't say:** *"Put it away, I won't repeat it."* (Sets up a fight.)

**Say:** *"Hey ___, I see your phone. I'm going to need it on the desk, screen down, until the end of class. We'll talk after. Thank you."*

Move on. Don't wait for compliance — move to the next thing in the lesson. Most students comply within 30 seconds when you don't make it a duel.

If they refuse: *"OK, we'll handle it after class. For now, please join the activity."* Then you genuinely move on.

### Script 2 — A student is openly disrespectful in front of the class

**Don't say:** *"Get out."* (Sometimes correct, but escalates and loses you the rest of the lesson.)

**Say:** *"That's not OK. We'll talk after class. Right now I need you to sit down and let us continue."*

Do not engage with the substance. *"Talk after class"* + *"sit down"* + *"continue"* — three phrases. Repeat them if needed. Don't add new ones.

After class: short, calm conversation. Not punishment, not lecture. Ask: *"What was going on?"* Listen. Then: *"Here's what I need from you tomorrow: ____."* End with a handshake or *"See you tomorrow."*

### Script 3 — A student is crying / shutting down / having a hard moment

**Don't say:** *"Are you OK?"* in front of the class. (They will say *yes* and shut down further.)

**Say (quietly, to them, while the rest of the class is busy):** *"Take a minute. Get water. Come back when you're ready."*

Send them to the bathroom or hallway. They come back when they're ready. You don't need a reason. They don't owe you an explanation.

After the lesson, find them quietly: *"Hey. Earlier — anything I should know?"* Sometimes you'll get a real answer. Sometimes *"I'm fine."* Both are valid responses. Don't push.

## When you go home crying

This will happen. It happens to every teacher.

### The 3-step end-of-day reset

1. **Close the door.** Literally. Sit. Phone away. 5 minutes of nothing.

2. **Write 3 lines:**
   - *One thing that went OK today: ____*
   - *One thing I'll fix tomorrow: ____*
   - *One thing that's not my fault: ____*

   The third line is the most important one. Some things are not your fault. The student whose home life you don't know about. The administrative mess. The class that was scheduled at 1:30pm right after lunch. Name them. Name them so you stop carrying them.

3. **Leave the building.** Don't keep working at your desk for another hour. Go home. Eat something. Talk to a non-teacher. Tomorrow is a new lesson.

### A letter to read on the worst days

> *Dear me on the bad day,*
>
> *Today's lesson did not work. That's not a verdict on you. It's a Tuesday.*
>
> *The 22 students who said nothing in class today are not your enemies. They are 14-year-olds. They are tired. Their parents fought last night. Their phone broke. Their friend posted something. They didn't sleep.*
>
> *The lesson plan you wrote was reasonable. The activity was reasonable. You showed up. You stood there. You tried.*
>
> *Tomorrow you do it again. Slightly different. Slightly better. That's the whole job.*
>
> *Five years from now you will be a great teacher because of what didn't work today, not in spite of it.*
>
> *Eat something. Watch something funny. Sleep.*
>
> *— Future you, who is fine.*

Photocopy this. Put it in your drawer.
