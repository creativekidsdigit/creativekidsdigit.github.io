## PAGE 2 — Understanding ADHD & Homework Struggles

**Headline (top):**
It's Not Defiance. It's a Brain Difference.

**Opening paragraph (body, calming):**
If homework time at your house feels like a battle, you are not alone — and you are not failing. ADHD brains are wired to find homework uniquely hard. Once you understand *why*, you can stop fighting your child's brain and start working with it.

**Subheading:**
Why Homework Is Harder for ADHD Kids

**Body bullets (short, scannable):**
- **Executive function** — the brain's "manager" — develops 2–3 years later in ADHD kids.
- **Working memory** is shorter. Instructions disappear seconds after they hear them.
- **Time blindness** is real. "20 minutes" feels the same as "2 hours."
- **Task initiation** is genuinely difficult — starting a task takes more energy than doing it.
- **Big emotions** flood the brain when a task feels overwhelming.

**Callout box (right column or boxed at bottom):**
> **A Reframe That Changes Everything**
>
> *Your child is not giving you a hard time.*
> *Your child is having a hard time.*

**Mini-checklist (boxed, "Common ADHD Homework Struggles — check what feels familiar"):**
☐ Procrastination that looks like laziness
☐ Meltdowns over small tasks
☐ Forgetting instructions seconds after hearing them
☐ Hyperfocus on the wrong thing
☐ Physical restlessness during seated work
☐ Big emotions when corrected
☐ "I can't do this" before they've started

**Closing line (italic, centered):**
*Whatever you checked — you're in the right place. Let's build a plan.*

**Layout direction:**
- 2-column structure: left = explainer text, right = checklist box
- Reframe callout in a tinted sage box with quote marks
- Soft brain or puzzle-piece icon next to headline

## PAGE 3 — Homework Routine Builder

**Headline:**
Build a Routine That Works *With* Their Brain

**Intro line:**
ADHD brains crave predictability. The same routine, in the same order, every day — that's the magic.

**Subheading:**
The 5-Block Homework Routine

**Five blocks displayed as horizontal cards (icon + title + description):**

🍎 **1. Reset Block** — *10 minutes*
Snack. Water. Movement. The brain needs fuel and a transition from school mode.

💛 **2. Connect Block** — *5 minutes*
Sit close. Ask one question about their day. *No homework talk yet.* Connection lowers resistance.

📋 **3. Plan Block** — *5 minutes*
Together, list what's due. Pick the order. Let *them* choose where to start.

✏️ **4. Work Block** — *15–25 minutes*
One task at a time. Visual timer running. You stay nearby.

🎉 **5. Reward Block** — *10 minutes*
Earned break. Celebrate effort, not just completion.

## PAGE 4 — ADHD-Friendly Study Environment Checklist

**Headline:**
Set the Space. Set Them Up to Succeed.

**Intro line:**
The right environment can cut homework resistance in half. Walk through this checklist together — let your child help choose what works.

**Subheading:**
Physical Setup Checklist

**Two-column checklist:**

*Column 1 — The Desk*
☐ Clear desk surface — only the current task visible
☐ Pencil, eraser, paper ready *before* starting
☐ Water bottle within reach
☐ Healthy snack nearby
☐ Visual timer in view
☐ Phone and tablet *out of sight* (not just face down)

*Column 2 — The Body & Senses*
☐ Fidget tool nearby (putty, spinner, stress ball)
☐ Wobble cushion or option to stand
☐ TV off, noise-cancelling headphones available
☐ Soft, warm lighting (avoid overhead fluorescents)
☐ Comfortable temperature
☐ "Do Not Disturb" sign for siblings

**Sensory mini-table ("Tune the Environment"):**

| Notice | Try |
|---|---|
| Too noisy? | Soft instrumental music or white noise |
| Too bright? | Warm lamp instead of overhead |
| Too cluttered? | Clear everything except the task |
| Too still? | Wobble cushion or standing desk |
| Too cold? | Hoodie, blanket, warm drink |

**Closing tip (callout box):**
> **One change at a time.**
> Change one thing. Try it for 3 days. Notice what helps. Then adjust.

## PAGE 5 — Focus Strategy Toolkit

**Headline:**
Tools to Help the Brain Stay With the Task

**Intro line:**
ADHD focus is not broken — it's just different. These six strategies are tested, science-backed, and parent-tested.

**Six strategy cards (2x3 grid):**

⏱️ **The 10-Minute Sprint**
Set a visual timer for 10 minutes. Work until it rings. Take a 2-minute movement break. Repeat. Short bursts beat long sessions.

🤝 **Body Doubling**
Sit nearby and do your own quiet task. Your calm presence helps regulate their focus. No nagging — just co-working.

📂 **One-Task-Visible Rule**
Cover other assignments with a folder or paper. The ADHD brain only needs to see *one* thing. Less visible = less overwhelming.

🦘 **Movement Anchors**
Standing desk. Bouncy ball seat. Walking while reciting facts. Movement *helps* an ADHD brain focus — it doesn't distract from it.

🗣️ **Read It Out Loud**
Reading the question aloud activates working memory. Whisper, sing, or use a silly voice — whatever helps.

🥇 **Hardest First — or Easiest First?**
Some kids: tackle the hard thing while fresh. Other kids: warm up with easy wins. Test both for one week each. Let *your* child's energy decide.

**Closing line (italic, centered):**
*You are not picking one strategy forever. You are building a toolbox.*

**Layout direction:**
- 2x3 grid of strategy cards
- Each card: icon at top, bold title, 2–3 sentence description
- Soft tinted backgrounds alternating between cream and sage
- Generous padding inside each card so it feels calm, not crowded

## PAGE 6 — Meltdown & Resistance Prevention Plan

**Headline:**
Catch It Before It Becomes a Storm.

**Intro line:**
Meltdowns rarely come from nowhere. The earlier you spot the warning signs, the easier it is to redirect.

**Subheading 1:**
Early Warning Signs (watch for these)

**Bulleted list:**
- Sighing, slumping, fidgeting more than usual
- Phrases like *"I can't"*, *"It's stupid"*, *"I hate this"*
- Tears welling, voice rising
- Pencil throwing, paper crumpling
- Going silent or shutting down

**Subheading 2:**
The 3 R's Response

**Three numbered cards:**

**1. Recognize**
*"I can see this is feeling really hard right now."*
Name what you see. No judgment. No fixing yet.

**2. Regulate**
*"Let's pause. Five deep breaths. Or a walk to the kitchen."*
Bodies first, brains second. Calm the nervous system before tackling the task.

**3. Re-engage**
*"When you're ready, we'll break it into smaller pieces."*
Return when *they* signal — not on your timeline. Then shrink the task.

**Subheading 3:**
What to Say (and What to Avoid)

**Two-column comparison:**

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Try Instead |
|---|---|
| "Why are you being like this?" | "Your brain is tired, not broken." |
| "Just focus." | "Let's just do the next 2 minutes." |
| "Your sister can do it." | "Your brain works in its own way." |
| "Stop crying and finish." | "It's okay to take a break. I'm here." |
| "You're being lazy." | "This is hard. Let's shrink it together." |

**Closing line (callout box):**
> **Your calm is contagious.**
> If you stay regulated, they will eventually borrow your calm.

**Layout direction:**
- Top: warning signs in a soft amber/peach tinted box
- Middle: 3 R's as numbered horizontal cards with large numerals
- Bottom: DO/DON'T table with red and green color accents
- Closing callout in a centered tinted box

## PAGE 7 — Brain Break Activity Page

**Headline:**
Quick Resets for Tired Brains

**Intro line:**
Brain breaks aren't a reward — they're a tool. A 2–5 minute reset can rescue an entire homework session.

**Four quadrants of activities:**

🏃 **Movement Breaks** *(2–5 min)*
- 10 jumping jacks
- Wall push-ups (count to 15)
- Animal walks — bear, crab, frog
- Dance to one full song
- Trampoline minute (or pretend!)
- Run upstairs and back

🌬️ **Calming Breaks**
- Cloud breathing — in for 4, out for 6
- Stretch like a cat, then a starfish
- Cold water on face and hands
- Squeeze a pillow tightly, then release
- Look out the window — count 5 things you see
- Hum your favorite song

🤲 **Sensory Breaks**
- Play with kinetic sand or putty
- Drink water through a thick straw
- Crunchy snack — carrots, apple, pretzels
- Weighted blanket cuddle (2 min)
- Smell lavender or peppermint
- Splash hands in cold water

🧠 **Brain Wake-Up Breaks**
- Solve 5 quick math problems in your head
- Recite a silly tongue twister
- Cross-body movements — left elbow to right knee
- Sing the alphabet backwards
- Name 5 animals starting with "S"
- Draw a picture in 60 seconds

**Closing line (italic):**
*Pick one. Set a timer. Come back ready.*

**Layout direction:**
- 2x2 quadrant grid, each quadrant a soft tinted box with a category icon
- Bullet list in each quadrant — keep tight spacing
- Small icons next to each activity (optional)

## PAGE 8 — Reward & Motivation System

**Headline:**
Motivation That Works for ADHD Brains

**Intro paragraph:**
Traditional star charts often fail ADHD kids — not because they don't want the reward, but because the reward is too far away. ADHD brains need motivation they can *feel right now*.

**Callout box (top, "Why Star Charts Often Fail"):**
- ADHD brains crave **immediate** rewards.
- Long-term goals feel invisible.
- Effort matters more than outcome.
- Every "missed star" feels like failure.

**Subheading:**
The 3-Tier Reward System

**Three stacked cards:**

⚡ **Micro Rewards** *(after each task — under 2 min)*
A high-five. A sticker. A fun-shaped pencil. 5 minutes of screen time. A piece of chocolate. Pick *with your child* before homework starts.

🎯 **Daily Reward** *(after homework is done)*
30 minutes of preferred activity. Choosing dinner. A bath with bubbles. A bedtime story chosen by them. Time outside.

🏆 **Weekly Reward** *(after 5 successful homework days)*
Family movie night. A small new toy or book. A trip to the park. Choosing the weekend breakfast. A friend over.

## PAGE 9 — Parent Communication Scripts

**Headline:**
The Right Words at the Right Moment

**Intro line:**
When your child is overwhelmed, your words become their script. These phrases lower resistance and rebuild connection.

**Five script categories, each as a tinted box:**

💬 **When they refuse to start**
- "Let's just look at the page together for 30 seconds."
- "You don't have to start yet. Sit down with me first."
- "What's the one tiny first step?"
- "Want me to read the first sentence out loud?"

💬 **When they say "I can't"**
- "You can't *yet*. That's different."
- "What part feels too hard? Let's shrink it."
- "I believe you it feels impossible. Let's make it smaller."
- "Show me the hardest part — we'll start there together."

💬 **When they're frustrated or in tears**
- "Your feelings make sense."
- "Let's pause. We'll come back."
- "I'm not going anywhere."
- "You don't have to be okay right now."

💬 **When they finish — celebrate effort, not outcome**
- "You stuck with it even when it was hard."
- "I noticed how you kept trying after that tough question."
- "That took real effort. I'm proud of how you tried."
- "Look what your brain just did."

💬 **When YOU are at your limit** *(say this to yourself)*
- *"He's not giving me a hard time. He's having a hard time."*
- "I need a 2-minute break. I'll be right back."
- "Let's both reset."
- "This is hard for both of us. We'll get through it."

**Closing line (italic, centered):**
*The words you choose become the voice in their head. Choose gently.*

## PAGE 10 — Emergency Homework Survival Plan

**Headline:**
When Everything Goes Wrong — A Plan

**Opening line (gentle, real):**
Some days, homework just won't happen. That's not failure. That's information. Here's what to do.

**Four numbered steps as vertical cards:**

🛑 **Step 1 — Stop the Bleeding**
☐ Close the books.
☐ Step away from the desk.
☐ Snack + water + 10 minutes of nothing.
☐ No lecturing. No "we'll deal with this later."

🩹 **Step 2 — Triage**
☐ What's due *tomorrow*? Do *only* that.
☐ What can wait? Skip it tonight without guilt.
☐ What can the teacher know about? Send a short note.

✉️ **Step 3 — A Note to the Teacher (use this template)**
> Hi [Teacher's name],
>
> [Child's name] had a difficult evening with homework tonight. We did our best with [the task we managed]. We weren't able to complete [the task we couldn't do]. We'll try again tomorrow with fresh energy. Thank you for your understanding.
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

💛 **Step 4 — Reconnect Before Bed**
☐ A hug.
☐ Read together — *anything*, even a comic book.
☐ Watch something funny.
☐ Tell them: *"Tomorrow is a fresh start."*

**Closing message (centered, larger, soft callout):**
> **A Note for You, Parent**
>
> You showed up.
> You tried again.
> You're not alone.
>
> This is the hardest unpaid job in the world —
> and you're doing it.
>
> Tomorrow: a fresh start.
> Tonight: rest.
